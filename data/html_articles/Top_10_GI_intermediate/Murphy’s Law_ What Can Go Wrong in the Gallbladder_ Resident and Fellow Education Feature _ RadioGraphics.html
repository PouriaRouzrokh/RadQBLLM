        Murphy’s Law: What Can Go Wrong in the Gallbladder: Resident and Fellow Education Feature

        Gallbladder disease affects approximately 6.3 million men and 14.2 million women aged 20–74 years in the United States. Ultrasonography (US), computed tomography (CT), and magnetic resonance (MR) imaging play a complementary role in evaluation of gallbladder disease and often lead to a definitive diagnosis. Management includes imaging follow-up, surgical or endoscopic intervention, and percutaneous drainage.

        Adenomyomatosis and gallbladder polyps are cholecystoses due to benign hyperplasia of cellular elements or accumulation of organic lipids in the gallbladder wall. They often are asymptomatic and incidentally discovered. Surgical management is indicated for polyps greater than 10 mm and in cases of primary sclerosing cholangitis. Findings of intramural cysts or reverberation artifact at US can help distinguish adenomyomatosis from other causes of gallbladder wall thickening.

        Evaluation for cholecystitis is the most frequent indication for gallbladder imaging. Acute calculous cholecystitis, the most common inflammatory condition affecting the gallbladder, is characterized by gallstones, wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid, and the presence of maximal abdominal tenderness over the visualized gallbladder at US when pressure is applied with the transducer (positive sonographic Murphy sign). Complications of gallbladder inflammation that may require emergent surgery include gangrene, emphysema, and hemorrhage. Gangrenous cholecystitis is due to ischemic necrosis of the gallbladder wall. Emphysematous cholecystitis is characterized by air within the gallbladder wall. Gallbladder hemorrhage manifests at imaging with blood products in the gallbladder lumen. Acalculous cholecystitis occurs primarily in critically ill patients and is due to increased bile viscosity that causes functional obstruction of the cystic duct. Other causes of gallbladder wall thickening include regional inflammation (eg, hepatitis) and systemic diseases (eg, heart failure and low-protein states).

        Chronic inflammatory conditions associated with gallstones include chronic and xanthogranulomatous cholecystitis. Chronic cholecystitis manifests with wall thickening at US and dysmotility at scintigraphy. An uncommon complication of chronic cholecystitis is porcelain gallbladder, which is characterized by mural calcifications at imaging. Xanthogranulomatous cholecystitis is a destructive process due to extravasation of bile into the gallbladder wall and accumulation of lipid-laden macrophages (Fig 1).

        Complications of cholelithiasis include gallstone ileus and Mirizzi syndrome. Gallstone ileus is caused by a fistula between the gallbladder and gastrointestinal tract and is a surgical emergency. Mirizzi syndrome is due to an impacted gallstone in the neck of the gallbladder or cystic duct, with obstruction of the common hepatic duct. Preoperative diagnosis with MR cholangiopancreatography or endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography is critical to avoid bile duct injury.

        Gallbladder cancer is an uncommon malignancy that is usually diagnosed at an advanced stage. It manifests as a mass or, less commonly, as focal or asymmetric wall thickening or a polyp. Gallbladder lymphoma is a rare entity that can mimic cholecystitis and is more commonly seen in systemic disease rather than as a primary lymphoma (Fig 2). Metastatic disease to the gallbladder is infrequent and usually due to melanoma.

        The online presentation provides an overview of these entities and combines clinical presentation, imaging findings, and appropriate pathologic correlation in a case-based multiple-choice question format. After viewing the presentation, radiologists should be able to identify imaging characteristics of various types of gallbladder disease, radiologic features that require follow-up or intervention, and diagnostic and therapeutic tools available to manage gallbladder disease.

        Abstract

        Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is a common condition and impairs the quality of life for millions of patients, accounts for considerable health care spending, and is a primary risk factor for esophageal adenocarcinoma. There have been substantial advances in understanding the pathogenesis of GERD and its complications and much progress in diagnosis and management of GERD; however, these have not been comprehensively discussed in the recent radiology literature. Understanding the role of imaging in GERD and its complications is important to aid in multidisciplinary treatment of GERD. GERD results from prolonged or recurrent reflux of gastric contents into the esophagus. Common symptoms include heartburn or regurgitation. Prolonged reflux of gastric contents into the esophagus can cause erosive esophagitis. Over time, the inflammatory response related to esophagitis can lead to deposition of fibrous tissue and development of strictures. Alternatively, the esophageal mucosa can undergo metaplasia (Barrett esophagus), a precursor to dysplasia (which can lead to adenocarcinoma). Conventional barium esophagography has long been considered the primary imaging modality for the esophagus, and the fluoroscopic findings for diagnosis of GERD have been well established. Multimodality imaging has a clear role in detection and assessment of the complications of GERD, specifically reflux esophagitis and Barrett esophagus; differentiation of benign and malignant strictures; and detection, staging, and posttreatment surveillance of esophageal adenocarcinoma. Given the dramatic changes in utilization of abdominal imaging during the past 2 decades, with significantly declining volume of fluoroscopic procedures and concomitant increase in CT and MRI studies, it is crucial that modern radiologists appreciate the value of barium esophagography in the workup of GERD and recognize the key imaging features of GERD and its complications at CT and MRI.

        • ■ List the complications of GERD and their histologic, endoscopic, and radiologic features.

        • ■ Discuss the current role of multimodality imaging in diagnosis and surveillance of GERD.

        Introduction

        Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is the most prevalent gastrointestinal disorder in the United States, affecting 15%–20% of the population (1). Between 2011 and 2015, medical expenditures for acid-suppressing drugs exceeded $60 billion, and in 2014, GERD accounted for more than 5.5 million ambulatory visits (2). This disease is not restricted to the United States, and it is estimated that one in 10 adults in the Western world experience reflux symptoms (1).

        Longitudinal epidemiologic studies show a direct relationship between obesity and GERD (3). In the United States, an obesity epidemic has been described and is believed to be responsible for the high prevalence of GERD in this country (4). Increased intra-abdominal pressure due to truncal obesity overwhelms the intrinsic pressure of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) or stimulates transient relaxation of the LES, allowing reflux of gastric contents into the distal esophagus (3).

        Prolonged exposure of the esophageal mucosa to refluxate initially causes inflammation (reflux esophagitis), the healing of which can lead to stricture development (5,6). Chronic reflux esophagitis can cause Barrett esophagus, in which the esophageal mucosa is replaced by intestinal mucosa (7,8). Barrett metaplasia predisposes to development of esophageal adenocarcinoma (7,8). GERD has been established as a strong risk factor for adenocarcinoma, and a rising incidence of GERD, largely attributed to increasing rates of abdominal obesity, has resulted in a rapid increase in the incidence of adenocarcinoma in developed countries (3).

        It is imperative to adequately diagnose and treat GERD and associated preneoplastic sequelae. Initial diagnosis is clinical, and there has been considerable progress in design and development of less invasive and more sensitive diagnostic tools and devices that can be deployed in an ambulatory setting, such as new sampling techniques (esophageal cytosponge) and salivary pepsin biomarker (9). High-resolution endoscopy provides exceptional mucosal detail, and technological advances in optical magnification and white-light and virtual chromoendoscopy (narrow-band imaging) have improved detection and characterization of esophageal disorders (10). Furthermore, novel approaches to endoscopic resection and fundoplication techniques have allowed minimally invasive treatment options (11).

        Although there is a limited role for multimodality imaging in diagnosis of GERD, there is a clear role for fluoroscopic esophagography in detection of the morphologic sequelae of GERD, including subtle mucosal changes associated with erosive esophagitis or Barrett esophagus, presence of strictures, or development of cancer (12). The primary imaging modality for evaluation of the esophagus remains fluoroscopy, but at many institutions this modality is no longer valued or emphasized—by referring clinicians and radiologists alike—with significant reduction in barium fluoroscopy studies performed (12,13). Decreased volumes and diminished interest have resulted in fluoroscopy studies primarily performed by fewer senior radiologists and diminished training for residents (14,15). This lack of adequate education, experience, and understanding of exceptional technique limits the ability to see the early subtle findings of mild to moderate reflux esophagitis or Barrett esophagus (16).

        Cross-sectional imaging is most useful for detection of GERD complications, specifically strictures (peptic or Barrett) and adenocarcinoma, although dynamic MRI has been shown to be feasible as a radiation-free imaging method for evaluation of swallowing with excellent accuracy (17–19). Esophageal luminal distention with effervescents has improved our ability to adequately visualize and characterize esophageal wall thickening, strictures, and masses, leading to improved specificity and accuracy of staging (19–22). PET/CT and MRI have been extensively studied for accuracy of staging, prediction of tumor response to therapy, and utility for recurrence surveillance (23–26).

        Given the rising incidence of GERD, it is crucial for radiologists to recognize the imaging features of reflux esophagitis and Barrett esophagus and identify their associated well-known complications, namely, strictures and adenocarcinoma. At the American Institute for Radiologic Pathology, we focus on the pathophysiologic understanding of disease as the basis for radiologic interpretation. This article is meant to provide a comprehensive and cohesive explanation of the pathophysiology of GERD and its associated complications, to improve detection and interpretation of relevant findings with all modalities.

        Normal Esophagus and Esophagogastric Junction

        Derived from the embryonic foregut, the esophagus is a long muscular tube connecting the oral cavity to the stomach. The esophagus is anatomically divided into three segments from cranial to caudal: cervical, thoracic, and abdominal (Fig 1). The cervical segment lies posterior to the trachea and anterior to the cervical spine; the thoracic segment descends in the posterior mediastinum, anterior to the thoracic spine; and the abdominal segment is usually less than 3 cm long and extends from the esophageal diaphragmatic hiatus to the esophagogastric junction (EGJ).

        Histologically, the esophagus is composed of four layers, from intraluminal to extraluminal: mucosa, submucosa, muscularis propria (consisting of the inner circular and outer longitudinal muscular layers), and adventitia (Figs 2, 3). Unlike the remaining hollow gastrointestinal tract, the esophagus contains a variable muscularis mucosa and lacks a continuous serosal layer, which allows tumors and infections to spread rapidly. The mucosa is composed of nonkeratinizing stratified squamous epithelium to the level of the cardia, where there is simple columnar epithelium, lamina propria, and muscularis mucosa (Figs 1, 2a). The squamocolumnar junction demarcates the transition from esophagus to stomach (Figs 1, 4).

        The primary function of the esophagus is to transport food from the oral cavity to the stomach. The swallow function is complex, involving central and peripheral inhibitory and excitation pathways, ultimately allowing coordinated peristalsis in the striated and smooth muscles of the cervical and thoracoabdominal esophagus, respectively (27). A supplementary function is to prevent involuntary regurgitation of gastric contents (27). This is achieved by a functional sphincter at the EGJ, which is composed of the contracted circular and longitudinal muscular layers and considered the internal LES (27). The crural diaphragm is anchored to the lower esophagus by the phrenoesophageal ligament and acts as an external sphincter (Fig 1).

        Combined, the internal and external sphincters are contracted at rest, resulting in high intraluminal pressure. These sphincters transiently relax with swallowing to allow passage of food into the stomach (Fig 4c) (27). Independent of swallowing, LES relaxation also occurs during belching, vomiting, and transient LES relaxation, mediated via the brainstem. Transient LES relaxation is evoked by gastric distention and stimulation of the subdiaphragmatic vagus afferents, and the relaxation is of longer duration than swallow-induced LES relaxation (27).

        Brief episodes of gastroesophageal reflux are physiologic, documented as early as the second trimester of development, and most occur in the postprandial state (28). These almost always follow transient LES relaxation, although they can also result from a transient increase in intra-abdominal pressure or spontaneous reflux through a hypotonic sphincter (29). Brevity and infrequency of the reflux episodes, an intact LES, and normal esophageal motility to clear the refluxate all protect against mucosal injury (6).

        Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease

        Definition and Classification

        By global consensus,

        GERD is defined as a condition that develops when reflux of gastric contents causes troublesome symptoms or esophageal mucosal injury or both (30).
        Patients who have mucosal injury with or without symptoms account for up to 40% of GERD cases in the United States; the other 60% of patients, who have symptoms without evidence of mucosal injury, are defined as having nonerosive reflux disease (NERD) (31).

        Clinical Manifestation

        The most characteristic symptoms of GERD are pyrosis (heartburn) and regurgitation. Pyrosis is characterized by painful retrosternal burning, usually of short duration. Some patients describe this as angina-type chest pain. Regurgitation is defined as involuntary and unexpected backflow of gastric contents into the mouth. Extraesophageal symptoms can also occur, and chronic cough, asthma, and chronic hoarseness have established associations with GERD. However, these extraesophageal symptoms rarely manifest in isolation, without concomitant heartburn or regurgitation (30).

        Pathophysiology

        The pathophysiology of GERD is multifactorial and complex. Symptoms and mucosal injury occur when the esophagus is exposed to frequent and prolonged episodes of acidic or bile salt refluxate, which is primarily associated with loss of LES function (6). This failure can result from hypotonia of the LES, increased transdiaphragmatic pressure gradient, or more frequent episodes of transient LES relaxation (Table 1) (6). Defective LES function is found in up to 85% of patients with GERD (32).

        In patients with a functioning LES, chronically increased intra-abdominal or intragastric pressure—due to obesity or delayed gastric emptying—can directly lead to reflux when the pressure exceeds that of the LES; or it can indirectly lead to reflux when there is increased stimulation of transient LES relaxation associated with gastric distention and vagal stimulation (3). Increased intra-abdominal pressure can also lead to proximal migration of the EGJ (hiatal hernia) with resultant loss of external sphincter function, weakening the LES. At diagnosis, poor esophageal motility can be considered primary if GERD is absent and a connective tissue or neurologic disorder exists or secondary in the presence of GERD (32).

        Diagnosis

        Symptomatic evaluation, preferably enhanced by structured questionnaires, remains the hallmark of initial diagnosis of GERD, despite general acceptance that this method has low sensitivity and specificity owing to symptomatic overlap with other esophageal disorders (33,34). Similarly, an empirical acid suppression test, or proton pump inhibitor (PPI) trial, has low diagnostic sensitivity (around 70%) and specificity (44%) (33,35). Regardless of how the patient is diagnosed with GERD, symptomatic relief using PPIs usually ensues. For those who do not respond to PPI therapy, endoscopy or esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD) is the most common next step to make a definitive diagnosis and assess for complications.

        When patients over the age of 50 years present with GERD symptoms, endoscopy may be warranted without the need for initial PPI trial owing to the higher risk of Barrett esophagus and esophageal adenocarcinoma in this cohort. High-resolution endoscopy provides exceptional mucosal detail, with improved detection and characterization of reflux-induced changes, presence of anatomic hiatal hernia, and reflux complications. High-definition endoscopy, enhanced with filters such as narrow-band imaging, can also allow detailed and enhanced depiction of subtle mucosal changes. However, most patients (almost 70%) with typical symptoms will have normal mucosa at endoscopy, and the sensitivity of EGD for diagnosis of GERD is low (36).

        Improved understanding of the pathophysiology of GERD has led to development of less invasive and more sensitive tools and devices, which allow ambulatory functional assessment of the esophagus, including documenting the presence of acidic reflux (ambulatory reflux monitoring using catheter-based or wireless pH probes) or nonacidic or weakly acidic reflux (pH probe with impedance measurement capabilities) (Fig 5). Novel functional probes measuring impedance in the lumen (functional lumen imaging probe [FLIP]) or along the surface (mucosal impedance [MI]) allow more precise assessment of luminal distensibility and mucosal integrity (37).

        Radiology has a limited role in initial diagnosis of GERD. Fluoroscopic esophagography is a readily available, safe, and inexpensive test that allows detection of spontaneous or provoked gastroesophageal reflux in patients with typical symptoms. However, recent studies comparing barium esophagography with pH monitoring have shown that the presence or absence of reflux documented at esophagography does not correlate with the presence or frequency of pathologic reflux, diminishing its value in diagnosis of GERD (36,38–40).

        Additionally, although high-volume reflux of barium (to or above the clavicles) was shown to correlate with recumbent pathologic reflux at ambulatory pH monitoring in a small retrospective study, there is no reference standard for how much reflux of barium (in terms of volume and proximal extent in the esophagus) must be observed for accurate diagnosis of GERD (41,42). Currently, the guidelines for diagnosis and management of GERD of the American College of Gastroenterology do not recommend barium studies as a diagnostic option (43,44): “Barium radiographs should not be performed to diagnose GERD (strong recommendation, high level of evidence).”

        However, beyond simply demonstrating gastroesophageal reflux, well-performed esophagography permits morphologic and functional assessment of the esophagus, providing a real-time view of the anatomy and function of the EGJ (Fig 4c). It is useful to delineate the anatomy of the esophagus and detect anatomic abnormalities, including hiatal hernia (12).

        Barium studies more accurately depict the presence, size, and type of hernias compared with endoscopy (42,45) (Fig 6).

        Hiatal hernias are commonly found in patients with severe GERD; although not all patients with hiatal hernia will have GERD, when present, the size of the hernia generally correlates with the severity of the injury (42). Hiatal hernias are also seen in patients with a shortened esophagus, an important factor to consider in preoperative workup of a patient because esophageal lengthening may be necessary for durable repair (12,45). Double-contrast esophagography allows detection of morphologic sequelae of GERD, including reflux esophagitis, peptic strictures, and Barrett esophagus (12). These features are described in detail later.

        Single-contrast esophagography is most useful in evaluation of the function of the esophageal body and LES and allows exclusion of other motility disorders that mimic GERD. Many patients with GERD will have esophageal dysmotility, depicted at esophagography as decreased or absent peristalsis without nonperistaltic contractions (46). Assessment of esophageal motility can also be performed with high-resolution manometry; however, in patients with complex or large hiatal hernia or in those unable to tolerate endoscopic catheter placement, barium studies are crucial (42).

        Other imaging modalities have been investigated in initial diagnosis of GERD in adults. Nuclear medicine esophageal scintigraphy can depict motility and the presence of reflux but is largely reserved for the pediatric population. At CT or MRI, gastroesophageal reflux can be inferred when contrast material is seen in the esophageal lumen, depending on the time of ingestion. Dynamic fast MR fluoroscopy during swallowing has been investigated as a noninvasive means of assessing the function and morphology of the esophagus and EGJ without ionizing radiation and has been shown to be feasible with promising results (17,18).

        Hiatal hernia can be seen as superior displacement of the stomach above the diaphragm at CT or MRI and may be mistaken for a posterior mediastinal or esophageal mass, depending on the degree of luminal distention (Fig 7a–7c). The type of hernia (paraesophageal or sliding) can frequently be determined, and multiplanar capability allows accurate measurement of the hiatal hernia in every plane (18,47–49).

        Treatment

        Adequate treatment of GERD is necessary for symptom relief and to prevent development of known GERD complications, specifically reflux esophagitis, strictures, Barrett esophagus, and esophageal adenocarcinoma. Treatment targets include improving the function and integrity of the LES and reducing the acidic content of the refluxate.

        Standard therapy involves both nonpharmacologic interventions, including dietary changes and lifestyle modifications, and pharmacologic interventions, using medications that (a) decrease the frequency of transient LES relaxation and improve esophageal motility, allowing improved clearance of acidic gastric contents, or (b) neutralize or reduce gastric acid production. The most important lifestyle modification that has been shown to significantly reduce GERD symptoms is weight loss (50,51).

        Endoscopic or surgical antireflux therapy is reserved for failure of medical management (refractory GERD symptoms, side effects of medications), quality of life issues (expense and inconvenience of lifelong therapy despite effective medical management), presence of GERD complications (peptic stricture, Barrett esophagus), or presence of large hiatal hernia distorting the EGJ and compromising LES function. Surgical options include fundoplication (laparoscopic or open, complete or partial) (Fig 7d). In obese patients, Roux-en-Y gastric bypass surgery can reduce reflux burden and GERD symptoms and can be performed initially, with simultaneous hernia repair if present, or after failed fundoplication (51,52). Sleeve gastrectomy can significantly worsen GERD symptoms, and GERD is a relative contraindication to the procedure (39,45,51).

        Newer minimally invasive laparoscopic and endoscopic luminal treatments have been introduced over the past 10 years, with improved symptom control and quality of life in patients with pathologic reflux symptoms, including transoral incisionless fundoplication, implantable magnetic sphincter augmentation of the LES, and implantable electrical stimulation of the LES (53,54); the last two involve implantable devices that are radiographically visible (Fig 8).

        Complications of GERD

        Reflux Esophagitis

        Definition and Pathophysiology.—

        The earliest and most common manifestation of injury due to gastroesophageal reflux is reflux esophagitis.
        Historically, direct caustic chemical injury (“burn”) of the surface epithelium due to exposure to refluxed acid and pepsin was believed to be the culprit in reflux esophagitis. However, more recent studies have identified an immune-mediated pathogenesis, where epithelial exposure to acid and bile stimulates secretion of proinflammatory cytokines, which induce a lymphocyte-predominant inflammatory response and basal cell proliferation (55,56). Ultimately, this inflammation mediates epithelial injury, manifested as surface erosions.

        Pathologic Features.—Histologically, reflux esophagitis manifests as inflammatory and regenerative changes in the esophageal mucosa. In the active inflammatory phase, there is infiltration of the epithelium by inflammatory cells and dilatation of intracellular spaces deep in the epithelium (Fig 9a). Regenerative changes include basal cell hyperplasia, elongation of lamina propria papillae, ballooned squamous cells, and dilatation of vascular channels. Ultimately, there is necrosis of surface squamous cells and focal areas of erosion. In the healing phase, surface erosions are absent, and there is deposition of collagen in the muscularis propria, which can lead to stricture development.

        At endoscopy, mucosal edema and inflammation can be depicted as reddened, erosive, or nodular mucosa (Fig 9b). Multiple small surface erosions are seen as punctate or linear ulcers, which progress to large flat superficial ulcers. To help guide management, the severity of erosive reflux esophagitis is graded on the basis of size and extent of mucosal breaks according to the Los Angeles classification (57).

        Grade A is defined as small mucosal breaks less than 5 mm in length. Grade B is defined as mucosal breaks equal to or greater than 5 mm that do not involve more than one mucosal fold. Grades C and D are defined as mucosal breaks in more than one fold and are distinguished by circumferential involvement (grade C is <75% involvement, grade D is >75% involvement). These ulcerative changes may be covered by coagulated exudate. In the early phases, edema and/or esophageal spasms can lead to luminal narrowing.

        Imaging Features.—The advent of double-contrast barium esophagography improved visualization of the early changes of reflux esophagitis compared with single-contrast studies. Thus, double-contrast esophagography remains the mainstay of radiologic assessment for reflux esophagitis (58). Owing to the mucosal edema seen in early reflux esophagitis, the mucosa of the distal esophagus appears finely nodular or granular, continuous with and extending proximally from the EGJ (Fig 9c). Edema can also manifest as thickening and irregularity of the longitudinal mucosal folds.

        An exaggerated focal mucosal inflammatory response can result in formation of a hyperplastic polyp, also referred to as an inflammatory esophagogastric polyp, seen at fluoroscopy as a smooth polyp arising from the gastric cardia and extending into the distal esophagus (59). Although reflux esophagitis is the most common cause of ulcers in the distal esophagus, these small surface erosions are not as well seen on double-contrast images; when present, they appear as punctate or linear collections of barium (12,16).

        Given the improvements in diagnosis and treatment, more advanced or severe disease is rarely encountered in modern practice today; however, when seen at single- or double-contrast esophagography, it appears as grossly irregular contour and luminal narrowing, associated with extensive inflammation, edema, and spasm (12). Extensive confluent ulceration involving longer segments can be depicted but should remain contiguous with the EGJ owing to the pathophysiology of reflux esophagitis. Distant ulcers in the proximal or mid esophagus, without contiguous disease, should suggest another cause of esophagitis (12,59).

        Although not indicated for reflux esophagitis, CT is routinely performed to evaluate for generalized or nonspecific abdominal pain, and findings of reflux esophagitis may be incidentally noted. During routine CT, the esophagus is usually collapsed or contains a small amount of air, which limits mucosal assessment but allows evaluation of wall thickness, enhancement, and involvement of surrounding structures (Fig 3a) (20,60). A recent study suggests that esophageal luminal distention of greater than 15–20-mm diameter or presence of an air-fluid level should be considered abnormal (60).

        Even in the collapsed state, esophageal wall thickness greater than 5 mm has been shown to correlate well with the presence of esophagitis, particularly when the target sign (mucosal enhancement with submucosal hypoattenuation due to edema) is also present (Fig 10b, 10c) (20,22).

        Adequate luminal distention is necessary to assess the esophageal mucosa and EGJ. Some centers have reported good results with multidetector CT performed immediately after the patient has ingested effervescent powder and water (virtual CT endoscopy or hydro–multidetector CT) or during continuous pump administration of carbon dioxide (pneumo-CT), with or without inducement of esophageal hypotonia by intramuscular injection of an antispasmodic agent, preferably with intravenous administration of contrast material (19,61–64). CT findings of reflux esophagitis correlate well with the histologic findings: specifically, we expect to see uniform circumferential wall thickening with mucosal enhancement and submucosal edema (20,65) (Fig 10a–10c).

        The feasibility of performing fast dynamic imaging during swallowing raises the possibility of developing MRI as a radiation-free tool to assess swallowing function and anatomy simultaneously (17,18,66). Although the mural architecture is not well delineated at conventional T1-weighted imaging, advances in the VIBE (volumetric interpolated breath-hold examination) technique have provided improved discrimination of the esophageal wall layers (Fig 3b–3e) (49,67,68). Esophageal mural architecture is best depicted on T2-weighted images, with the normal mucosa seen as a thin often corrugated layer of intermediate signal intensity surrounded by high-signal-intensity submucosa with an outer thin layer of low-signal-intensity muscularis propria (Fig 3e) (67,69,70). Although MRI characteristics of reflux esophagitis have not yet been specifically studied, extrapolating from extensive research on other gastrointestinal inflammatory diseases manifesting with mural edema and similar to CT, MRI would demonstrate esophageal wall thickening and submucosal edema (Fig 10d) (71).

        Treatment.—As with GERD without endoscopic or radiologic evidence of reflux esophagitis, the primary treatment algorithm for patients with reflux esophagitis combines lifestyle, dietary modifications, and daily PPI therapy. A standard treatment cycle has been shown to heal erosive esophagitis in up to 86% of patients (72). Patients with severe erosive esophagitis may require long-term maintenance acid suppression. The endoscopic and surgical options described earlier can be useful for patients who cannot tolerate long-term medical therapy, have disease progression despite medical management, have a large hiatal hernia, or are obese.

        Peptic Stricture

        Definition and Pathophysiology.—Reflux-induced strictures, also called peptic strictures, account for 90% of benign esophageal strictures, and GERD is the most common cause of stricture in the distal esophagus (73,74). Concurrent with increasing use of PPI therapy for GERD, the incidence of peptic esophageal stricture has steadily decreased; peptic strictures are seen in 1%–2% of patients with GERD (73).

        Peptic strictures result from the healing process of chronic erosive reflux esophagitis. During the healing process, collagen is haphazardly deposited in the submucosa and muscularis propria. Over time, these fibrous bands can become confluent around the circumference of the esophagus and contract, symmetrically or asymmetrically constricting the esophageal lumen, depending on the placement and extent of fibrous proliferation.

        Patients may experience solid food dysphagia and food impaction; 80% of patients with peptic stricture have dysphagia. Peptic strictures are identified in almost 10% of patients with GERD and dysphagia (42,73).

        Pathologic Features.—Histologically, peptic strictures are characterized by the presence of fibrosis in the muscularis propria, frequently with coexistent inflammatory and regenerative histologic changes of reflux esophagitis (Fig 11a). Most commonly, these involve short segments of the distal esophagus, contiguous or adjacent to the squamocolumnar junction (Fig 11b).

        Endoscopy allows identification of a peptic stricture as a focal area of narrowing of the distal esophagus, usually located at or near the squamocolumnar junction (Fig 11c). There is a decreased mucosal vascular pattern, and the segment is difficult to distend with air insufflation. However, endoscopy is insensitive and can fail to demonstrate subtle areas of narrowing, where the lumen is or exceeds 10 mm in diameter (42). Alternatively, tight strictures can be difficult to cross, compromising full assessment of length. Nevertheless, when narrowing is recognized, endoscopy has the added benefit of concurrent treatment with dilation.

        Imaging Features.—Biphasic esophagography is more accurate than endoscopy in detecting peptic strictures, given the ability of this study to demonstrate even subtle areas of narrowing, with sensitivity approaching 95% (42,58,75). American Gastroenterological Association (AGA) guidelines recommend barium esophagography for the GERD patient with dysphagia because it allows one to “evaluate for complications of GERD, including strictures or rings” (43,44). For characterization and treatment purposes, it is most important to describe the location, length, and character of the stricture.

        At esophagography, peptic strictures are classically concentric segments of luminal narrowing with smooth contours and tapered margins, measuring 1–4 cm in length (Fig 11d). They are frequently associated with hiatal hernia, possibly owing to concomitant shortening of the distal esophagus, retracting the EGJ superiorly (59). Asymmetric deposition of fibrous tissue can result in eccentric luminal narrowing or the appearance of sacculations, in which intraluminal contrast material is trapped between fibrous bands (12). Intramural pseudodiverticulosis (a focal outpouching of contrast material filling dilated excretory ducts of submucosal glands) may be associated with peptic strictures and is best seen at single-contrast esophagography (Fig 11d) (12,74).

        Although double-contrast esophagography and endoscopy are the primary modalities for evaluating the esophagus for stricture, CT with multiplanar capabilities can be helpful in noninvasively differentiating benign and malignant strictures, enhanced by using the virtual endoscopy technique described earlier. Chronic inflammatory change and deposition of fibrosis associated with chronic reflux esophagitis result in homogeneous wall thickening with gradual narrowing of the esophageal lumen (76). This results in cone-shaped suprastenotic dilatation with smooth obtuse margins, best seen on multiplanar reconstructions in the coronal or sagittal plane (76).

        Intravenous contrast material is helpful in showing arterial enhancement of the intact mucosa, with relative hypoenhancement of the submucosa (target sign) due to inflammation and edema (Fig 10b, 10c). Progressive mural enhancement has been shown and is presumably due to abundant fibrous stroma (76). The surrounding fat planes are preserved, and there should be no lymphadenopathy (21,76). MRI should yield similar findings, but this has not yet been studied, presumably owing to the added cost and limited availability of MRI.

        Treatment.—The therapeutic approach for a patient with peptic stricture depends on the characteristics of the stricture, but it generally relies on endoscopic dilation with concomitant PPI therapy (54,73). Acid suppression with PPIs can treat the underlying GERD and protect against stricture recurrence. Simple strictures can undergo endoscopic dilation. More complex strictures, such as those associated with large hiatal hernias, defined by length greater than 2 cm and tortuous and irregular course, may respond to endoscopic dilation but may require repeated sessions.

        Refractory strictures are rare but pose a significant problem, in that successful treatment options are limited. Direct endoscopic injection of corticosteroids into the stricture site, endoscopically placed esophageal stents, and endoscopic incisional therapy have been used, but the efficacy of these procedures is not well established (11).

        Barrett Esophagus

        Definition and Pathophysiology.—In the United States, Barrett esophagus is defined as intestinal metaplasia of the esophageal squamous epithelium (transformation of squamous epithelium into intestinalized columnar epithelium) in response to chronic reflux esophagitis (Fig 12) (7,10).

        In 10%–15% of patients with chronic mucosal injury associated with gastroesophageal reflux, metaplasia of the esophageal stratified squamous epithelium can occur as a protective or healing mechanism (5). It is unclear if mature squamous cells chronically inflamed by GERD transform into columnar cells (transdifferentiation) or if chronic GERD induces differentiation of immature stem cells into intestinal columnar cells (transcommitment), but it is clear that the duration of reflux is the most important determinant for development of Barrett esophagus (7,8).

        The mucus-secreting columnar cells that replace the squamous epithelium are resistant to reflux-induced injury, and intestinal columnar metaplasia causes no symptoms; however, this metaplasia predisposes the patient to development of esophageal adenocarcinoma. Barrett esophagus is a well-established premalignant condition that serves as the first step in the progression from metaplasia to low-grade dysplasia, high-grade dysplasia, and adenocarcinoma (7).

        Pathologic Features.—In the United States and most other countries, diagnosis of Barrett esophagus with histologic analysis requires the presence of intestinal metaplasia with goblet cells (Figs 12, 13a) (77). A complete histologic evaluation requires description of the degree of dysplasia (absent, low-grade, or high-grade) as determined by surface preservation, nuclear stratification, and glandular architecture (Fig 13b, 13c) (77).

        Endoscopic correlation that histologically confirmed goblet cell–containing columnar mucosa extends above the EGJ and lines the esophagus is required for diagnosis (77). The columnar mucosa is described as salmon colored and has a velvety texture that is distinct from the smooth, pale, and glossy appearance of the normal squamous mucosa (Figs 11c, 14a, 14b) (7,77). It is important for the endoscopist to define the extent of columnar metaplasia, typically by documenting the circumferential and maximum extent of the metaplasia (the circumference and the maximal distance between the EGJ and the squamocolumnar junction [Z line]) (10). This is because long-segment Barrett esophagus (defined as >3 cm) has the greatest potential to develop into adenocarcinoma and thus should be identified (30).

        Imaging Features.—Double-contrast esophagography has low sensitivity and specificity for identifying Barrett esophagus (12,59). The classic finding at esophagography is a stricture or ulcer in the midesophagus, thought to arise at the elevated squamocolumnar junction of long-segment Barrett esophagus (Fig 14c, 14d) (12). However, these findings are seen in only 5%–10% of patients with Barrett esophagus (59). Fluoroscopy can be helpful in determining the risk for Barrett esophagus on the basis of specific criteria.

        Patients with the classic finding of a midesophageal stricture or ulcer are considered at high risk for Barrett esophagus; patients with typical findings of reflux esophagitis and a stricture in the distal esophagus would be considered at moderate risk (12). Both high-risk and moderate-risk patients should undergo endoscopy, depending on their appropriateness for surveillance. However, most patients fall into the low-risk category, having no evidence of reflux esophagitis or strictures, and these patients benefit most from empirical treatment without the need for endoscopy (59).

        Treatment.—Since diagnosis of Barrett esophagus requires endoscopy and biopsy, the current recommended strategy is endoscopic screening in patients who have chronic GERD symptoms and at least one other risk factor for adenocarcinoma (7,10). Risk factors for adenocarcinoma include male sex, age older than 50 years, white ethnicity, hiatal hernia, abdominal obesity, and a history of tobacco abuse (10,78). However, it must be recognized that limiting the target population to only those with chronic GERD will miss 40% of patients with adenocarcinoma, which means that current recommended screening strategies should be reconsidered (7,10,78).

        Recognition of the limitations of endoscopy as a high-volume screening tool has led to development of novel technologies that can evaluate for Barrett metaplasia and are more suited to ambulatory care, such as reusable wireless or tethered capsule–based imaging using volume laser endomicroscopy, which can image the surface and subsurface to 3-mm depth; esophageal cell collection devices, like an encapsulated sponge, which when swallowed can collect large numbers of esophageal mucosal cells that can be assessed for the presence of Barrett esophagus–specific biomarkers; and blood-based screening tests, which can measure circulating microRNAs, small noncoding RNA molecules present in patients with Barrett esophagus (9). However, until large-scale trials can confirm the accuracy, acceptability, and cost-effectiveness of these devices and tests, white-light endoscopy remains the reference standard for diagnosis of Barrett esophagus (7,10).

        When dysplasia is discovered in Barrett esophagus, patients will undergo routine surveillance and/or endoscopic eradication therapy, depending on the pathologic grade (7,10,79). High-grade dysplasia progresses to adenocarcinoma rapidly enough to warrant intervention. Until recently, esophagectomy was the standard therapy, but newer endoscopic resection and ablation techniques have been shown to be safe and efficacious in elimination of all intestinal metaplasia to prevent progression to adenocarcinoma and restore normal-looking squamous mucosa, thus significantly lowering morbidity and mortality (7).

        Esophageal Adenocarcinoma

        Definition and Pathophysiology.—Esophageal cancer is the seventh most common malignancy and the sixth leading cause of cancer death worldwide, resulting in over 500 000 deaths in 2018 (80). The two most common histologic subtypes are squamous cell carcinoma and adenocarcinoma. Historically, squamous cell carcinoma was the most common pathologic type globally; however, in Western countries, the incidence of adenocarcinoma is increasing with a concomitant decrease in squamous cell carcinoma; thus, adenocarcinoma is the most common subtype of esophageal cancer in high-income countries (eg, United States, Australia, France, United Kingdom) (80,81). The rapid rise in the incidence of adenocarcinoma in Western countries is largely attributed to the increasing prevalence of abdominal obesity, which contributes to development of GERD and Barrett esophagus, the two major causes of esophageal adenocarcinoma (80,81).

        Symptomatic patients most commonly report new-onset rapidly progressive dysphagia and weight loss. Unfortunately, early adenocarcinoma is rarely associated with symptoms, and dysphagia is associated with advanced disease (82).

        Worldwide, the prognosis of esophageal adenocarcinoma is poor owing to the late presentation of symptoms and the aggressiveness of the tumor (83). The most important prognostic factor is tumor stage. Improvement in detection of earlier-stage tumors, due to increased awareness and surveillance of patients with Barrett esophagus, and improvement in treatment options have resulted in modest improvements in survival (5-year survival has increased from 5% to 20% since the 1960s) (78,81).

        Pathophysiologically, the precursor lesion for esophageal adenocarcinoma is Barrett esophagus. However, the progression of Barrett esophagus to esophageal adenocarcinoma is incompletely understood, and identification of specific biomarkers to better determine the risk of progression is the subject of ongoing research (10,83–85). It has been shown that the intestinal metaplastic epithelium is hyperproliferative, and cellular proliferation increases as the epithelium undergoes dysplastic changes (86). Continued intermittent exposure to refluxate (ongoing GERD) further stimulates hyperproliferation, presumably owing to direct cytotoxic effects of refluxed acid and bile and chronic inflammation, which have been linked to cancer development (8).

        Pathologic Features.—High-grade dysplasia is considered a tumor in situ and is histologically defined as loss of surface architecture, loss of nuclear stratification, abnormal nuclei (round, enlarged, hyperchromatic), and glandular crowding and distortion (Fig 13c) (77). As the abnormal glands infiltrate the mucosa and submucosa, there is increased crowding (back-to-back glands) with the presence of single cells and small clusters, indicative of intramucosal and submucosal adenocarcinoma (Fig 15a). More advanced tumors may demonstrate a tubular, papillary, or mucinous growth pattern, with signet ring cells seen only rarely (77).

        Differentiation is graded by the amount of gland formation, and the degree of nuclear atypia generally follows the grade of differentiation (77). The esophageal mucosa has a robust and complex network of lymphatics; consequently, infiltration into regional lymph nodes occurs early and frequently (87). The absence of a proper serosa allows easy invasion into adjacent structures (88).

        At endoscopy, these tumors are usually detected within or associated with areas of Barrett esophagus and can initially appear as flat mucosal nodules (Fig 15b). More advanced tumors are bulky and have infiltrative circumferential growth that may cause strictures (Fig 15c).

        Imaging Features.—The role of multimodality imaging in esophageal adenocarcinoma will vary according to the presentation (detection, staging, and/or posttreatment monitoring). More than 90% of cases have no preceding diagnosis of Barrett esophagus; these cases are most commonly diagnosed incidentally via imaging performed for unrelated symptoms or diagnosed in symptomatic patients at a late stage, as symptoms are typically associated with advanced disease (83,89). Once the diagnosis is made, accurate staging requires determination of the location, length, and depth of the tumor; the number and locations of lymph nodes; and the presence of distant metastases (90). After treatment, multimodality imaging plays a role in assessing treatment response and identifying recurrent, progressive, or metastatic disease. Complementary use of the different imaging modalities maximizes the accuracy of staging and therapeutic monitoring, and it is important to recognize the advantages and disadvantages of each technique.

        Endoscopy.—Endoscopy with US is considered the most accurate imaging modality for initial diagnosis and clinical tumor (T) staging of esophageal cancer owing to the ability to directly visualize the individual layers of the wall, allowing assessment of tumor depth with 80%–92% accuracy (62). Endoscopic mucosal resection of superficial tumors not only provides the precise depth of tumor invasion and histologic assessment of grade but also the extent of lymphovascular invasion, all of which can be used to guide effective management. In more advanced tumors, endoscopy with US can readily depict involvement of the muscularis propria (T2 and T3 tumors) (Fig 15d). However, larger tumors (T4) may extend beyond the depth of sound wave penetration, limiting assessment of the full tumor depth and invasion of adjacent structures. Endoscopy can be limited by high operator dependency, restricted field of view, inability to pass obstructing tumors (<5 mm luminal diameter), and relative invasiveness requiring sedation, which may not be clinically appropriate for all patients.

        Fluoroscopy.—Esophagography is usually the initial noninvasive imaging study in patients with dysphagia (74). The presence of dysphagia is associated with more advanced esophageal adenocarcinoma, although malignant masses can be detected with esophagography at earlier stages as well, albeit with poor sensitivity (<40%) (91). Double- or single-contrast esophagography can be used for initial detection of tumor in symptomatic patients but has limited utility in staging esophageal cancer and is not included in NCCN (National Comprehensive Cancer Network) workup guidelines (92,93).

        The earliest morphologic manifestation of esophageal cancers reflects early mucosal changes, where the surface architecture flattens and can be seen as a flat plaque-like lesion (91). As the tumor involves the submucosa, a masslike protrusion is often observed, with or without ulceration (Fig 16a) (91). With more advanced disease, esophageal tumors can appear masslike, polypoid, ulcerative, or infiltrative (Fig 16b) (74,89).

        Infiltrative tumors may produce malignant strictures, which can be differentiated from benign strictures by an irregular nodular contour and abrupt “shouldered” margins (Fig 16c) (58,94). Dysphagia typically occurs once the lumen has been narrowed by 50%–75% (95). As the tumor invades surrounding structures, including the airway or mediastinum, fistulous tracts may develop with adjacent structures such as the trachea, bronchi, and pleural space. When a fistulous tract is clinically suspected, water-soluble esophagography may delineate the fistula (89).

        Computed Tomography.—CT can be performed to clarify findings seen at esophagography and assess the location, size, and extent of invasion of the primary tumor, as well as identify regional lymph nodes and detect distant metastases; NCCN guidelines recommend CT of the chest and abdomen as a core component of staging esophageal adenocarcinoma (92). CT is well tolerated by most patients and is readily available at most health care systems. Disadvantages of CT include radiation exposure, impossibility of obtaining biopsy or cytologic material, and inability to treat stenosis.

        When adequate luminal distention is achieved, CT can be a useful modality for assessing the esophageal lumen and wall, especially when viewed in multiple planes. Studies analyzing the efficacy and utility of multidetector CT performed after ingestion of effervescents have shown improved sensitivity for detection of tumors (62,96), accuracy of tumor staging, and accuracy of nodal staging (62,95,96).

        Ideally, CT is performed with gaseous luminal distention and intravenous contrast material, which improve characterization of total tumor length and margins and allow assessment of mucosal integrity, luminal caliber, and intraluminal projections (Fig 17a, 17b). However, the esophageal wall, surrounding fat planes, regional and distant lymph nodes, and adjacent organs can be characterized without use of effervescents or intravenous contrast material (21,63,95). Three-dimensional reconstructions can better depict the location and length of the stricture, the luminal diameter, and the lumen beyond the narrowed segment for diagnostic and preoperative planning (Fig 17c) (21,95).

        CT findings reflect the local growth of the adenocarcinoma (Table 2, Fig 18) (19). Specifically, early adenocarcinoma can manifest as mild focal asymmetric or circumferential wall thickening (>5 mm) or an intraluminal polypoid mass, with preservation of the surrounding fat plane. As the tumor invades the muscularis propria (T2), there is increased diffuse or circumferential wall thickening (up to 15 mm) (19). However, T2 tumor remains confined to the wall, and the outer margin of the esophagus remains smooth without infiltration of surrounding fat (63).

        T1 and T2 tumors are not usually associated with stricture (76). Contrast enhancement can improve detection and characterization of these early tumors (Fig 17b). Esophageal carcinoma is typically hypervascular, and the presence of avid arterial enhancement has high sensitivity, specificity, and accuracy (all >80%) for esophageal adenocarcinoma (76).

        Transmural invasion into the adventitia is characteristic of a T3 tumor and manifests at CT as increasing wall thickening (>15 mm), infiltration of surrounding fat, and luminal narrowing (Figs 17b, 17c, 19). Malignant strictures can be differentiated from benign strictures with CT when eccentric wall thickening, irregular or nodular margins, disrupted mucosal surface enhancement, and abrupt transition with cup-shaped suprastenotic dilatation are present (Fig 17c) (76).

        As the tumor continues to grow, there is invasion of the adventitia and surrounding structures, depicted at CT as loss of the fat plane between the esophagus and adjacent structures, and local invasion (T4). CT has high sensitivity and specificity for detecting mediastinal invasion and adjacent organ involvement, reported to be as high as 90%–100% and 92%–95%, respectively, and a preoperative CT study with negative results is a reliable indicator of resectability (47). It is crucial to determine whether invasion into vital structures (aorta, vertebral body, or airway) has taken place; if invasion has occurred, this stage T4b disease is considered unresectable. Local invasion into the mediastinum, tracheobronchial tree, pleura, or diaphragm may manifest as an esophageal fistula, depicted at CT as a gas- or contrast material–containing sinus tract.

        Lymphadenopathy is seen in 74%–88% of patients with esophageal adenocarcinoma, mostly owing to the robust lymphatic network (19). Reliance on size as the primary criterion for nodal disease is insensitive because malignant lymph nodes are not necessarily enlarged, and inflamed or reactive lymph nodes can be swollen (90). Morphologic assessment of nodal shape, hilar fat, and enhancement can help improve sensitivity.

        In addition to local invasion and lymphatic dissemination, esophageal adenocarcinoma can spread via hematogenous embolization or transperitoneal seeding. The most common site for hematogenous spread is the liver owing to portal venous drainage of the distal esophagus. Other sites include the lungs, adrenal glands, kidneys, bones, and brain. Transperitoneal seeding is seen in the case of more advanced disease. CT can be used to detect metastases, although with less sensitivity and accuracy than fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) PET.

        Positron Emission Tomography.—To date, PET/CT is the most sensitive and accurate imaging modality for detection of distant metastases (Fig 20), and findings at PET/CT can change management decisions in up to 38% of patients (97–99). Current guidelines recommend PET/CT to detect distant metastatic (stage IV) disease when standard staging methods suggest the presence of locally advanced resectable disease or after neoadjuvant chemoradiation therapy before surgery to confirm absence of distant lymphatic and hematogenous metastases (99).

        Fluorine 18 (18F)–FDG PET has also been used to detect response to neoadjuvant chemoradiation therapy, although meta-analyses of studies using maximum standardized uptake value (SUV) have shown heterogeneous results (26,100,101). New research has focused on use of texture analysis at 18F-FDG PET to predict tumor response to neoadjuvant therapy before treatment initiation (102,103). Textural features, such as metabolic tumor volume (MTV) and total lesion glycolysis (TLG), have been shown to independently allow prediction of tumor response, providing useful information for personalized patient management (104–106).

        Finally, 18F-FDG PET/CT has been shown to accurately demonstrate symptomatic distant recurrence of esophageal cancer with high sensitivity and moderate specificity (25,107).

        MR Imaging.—The high spatial resolution capability of MRI enables noninvasive visualization of the esophageal wall and surrounding structures without ionizing radiation, allowing precise evaluation of depth of mural invasion concurrent with broader-field assessment of regional lymph nodes and distant metastases (68,108,109). There is continued work on maximizing T1- and T2-weighted sequences for highest diagnostic accuracy for depth of invasion (Table 2, Fig 21) (68,69,108,110,111). Recent recognition of a periesophageal connective tissue plane, the so-called mesoesophagus, which can be depicted at MRI, has led to development of a minimally invasive mesoesophageal resection model, similar to the mesorectal excision model for rectal cancer (112,113). Current challenges to be overcome for more widespread clinical application include minimizing motion artifact associated with cardiac and diaphragmatic motion and the high cost of MRI.

        Functional imaging can also be performed using MRI. Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) and dynamic contrast enhancement (DCE) demonstrate microstructural changes in pathologic tissue and allow quantification of tissue parameters, specifically changes in interstitial volume and cellular permeability (DWI) and vascular permeability (DCE). Both improve detection of the primary tumor and local-regional lymph nodes (staging) (111).

        Changes in the apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) derived from DWI have been shown to accurately predict tumor response to neoadjuvant treatment (114). Furthermore, because higher pretreatment ADCs are seen in nonresponders (presumably owing to the presence of intratumoral necrosis, which is unresponsive to cytotoxic treatments), patients with low-ADC tumors are predicted to have greater benefit from neoadjuvant chemoradiation therapy. Patients with high-ADC tumors may benefit more greatly from immediate surgical resection, while avoiding side effects from ineffective neoadjuvant chemotherapy (111).

        When hybrid MRI–radiation therapy systems are used, the high tissue contrast resolution of MRI allows accurate delineation of tumor margins for real-time visualization and tracking during radiation therapy.

        Treatment.—Accurate staging is crucial when making treatment decisions; it is important to provide the patient with the best chance of survival, while minimizing harm from over- or undertreatment.

        Endoscopic resection and ablation are the preferred treatments for early-stage adenocarcinoma (tumor in situ [pTis] or stage T1a without nodal disease [N0]), although definitive esophagectomy is considered the treatment of choice for tumors that deeply invade the submucosa (pT1b) (79). Medically fit patients with clinically staged tumors typically undergo neoadjuvant chemoradiation therapy before esophagectomy, although patients with smaller low-risk tumors (cT1b–T2) may proceed straight to esophagectomy (92). Patients with unresectable tumors (T4b tumors with direct invasion into vital organs) or nonsurgical candidates undergo definitive chemoradiation therapy (92).

        Conclusion

        More than 15% of Americans experience regular GERD symptoms. GERD can be complicated by reflux esophagitis, which predisposes to stricture formation, and Barrett esophagus, which predisposes to esophageal adenocarcinoma.

        Although initial diagnosis of GERD does not rely on radiologic studies, multimodality imaging has a crucial role in recognition of reflux esophagitis, characterization of strictures, stratification of patients at high risk for developing Barrett esophagus, and staging and surveillance of esophageal adenocarcinoma.

        The primary modality for evaluating the esophagus is still double-contrast esophagography. American Gastroenterological Association (AGA) guidelines recommend barium esophagography for the GERD patient with dysphagia because it best enables evaluation for complications of GERD, including strictures. Dysphagia is a common complaint of GERD patients, and the prevalence of GERD is rising in the Western world. This translates into an increasing number of patients who need esophagography as part of their care.

        Therefore, it is crucial that practicing radiologists are able to provide high-quality studies that can be inherited and improved on by future physicians. As fewer modern radiologists are exposed to esophageal fluoroscopic studies, practical experience and technical expertise decrease, thereby diminishing the diagnostic accuracy of and clinician’s confidence in these studies. It is imperative that we continue to address educational gaps in training and collaborate with our gastroenterology colleagues to demonstrate the continued added value of fluoroscopy in the workup and management of GERD.

        Similarly, with increasing volumes of CT and MRI studies, it is crucial that modern radiologists are aware of the imaging appearance of GERD and its complications. Multimodality imaging has an important role in characterization and differentiation of benign and malignant strictures, and efforts to improve cross-sectional imaging of the esophagus—including virtual CT endoscopy and dynamic fast MRI—are ongoing. There is a clear role for multimodality imaging (CT, PET/CT, and MRI) in staging, posttreatment assessment, and postsurgical surveillance of esophageal adenocarcinoma.

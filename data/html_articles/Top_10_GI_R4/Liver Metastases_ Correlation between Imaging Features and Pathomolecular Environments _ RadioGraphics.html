        Abstract

        A wide range of imaging manifestations of liver metastases can be encountered, as various primary cancers preferably metastasize to the liver (organ-specific metastases), with the imaging characteristics largely depending on various primary tumor–specific factors such as histopathologic category, degree of tumor differentiation, histologic behavior, and intratumor alterations. Characteristic imaging features potentially can help provide a more precise diagnosis in some clinical settings. These settings include those of (a) primary cancers of hollow organs such as gastrointestinal organs, the lungs, and the bladder, owing to the appearance of metastases that cannot be applied to the liver, which is a parenchymal organ; (b) unknown primary tumors; (c) more than one primary tumor; (d) another emergent malignancy; and (e) transformation to a different histopathologic tumor subtype. The characteristic features include the target sign on T2-weighted MR images or during the hepatobiliary phase of hypovascular metastasis, the peripheral rim washout sign on delayed phase images, peritumor hyperintensity during the hepatobiliary phase, hypervascular metastasis, a cystic appearance with marked hyperintensity on T2-weighted images, marked hyperintensity on T1-weighted images, calcification, capsular retraction, absence of the vessel-penetrating sign, distribution of liver metastases, and rare intraductal forms of metastases. In addition to various factors associated with the primary cancer, desmoplastic reactions around the tumor—which can be observed in adenocarcinomas with peripheral and peritumor enhancement, distinct arterioportal shunts with metastases from pancreatic ductal carcinoma, and pseudocirrhosis—also can affect these findings. The authors review the characteristic imaging findings of liver metastases from various primary cancers, with a focus on the mechanisms that underlie organ-specific liver metastases.

        SA-CME LEARNING OBJECTIVES

        After completing this journal-based SA-CME activity, participants will be able to:

        • ■ Describe the mechanisms underlying organ-specific liver metastases and the role of tumor exosomes in formation of premetastatic niches.

        • ■ Discuss how the desmoplastic reactions produced by cancer-associated fibroblasts explain peripheral and peritumor enhancement of colorectal liver metastases, arterioportal shunts accompanying pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma metastases, and pseudocirrhosis.

        • ■ List the characteristic imaging findings of liver metastases from specific primary cancers.

        Introduction

        The liver is one of the most common sites of metastases, and liver metastases are more common than primary liver cancers (1–3). A large number of malignancies—including cancers of the gastrointestinal system (eg, colorectum, stomach, pancreas, biliary), genitourinary system (eg, kidney, prostate), gynecologic system (eg, ovary, endometrium, cervix), neuroendocrine system, and breast; soft-tissue sarcomas; melanomas; and lymphoma (4)—tend to metastasize to the liver.

        As patients with liver metastases have a worse prognosis than patients who do not have them (1), accurate diagnosis is crucial for appropriate management of these secondary cancers. Imaging findings have an important role in facilitating accurate evaluations to detect and characterize metastases. Thus, the number, location, and size of liver metastases need to be accurately determined. In addition, assuming that there may be cases of more than one primary cancer, new primary cancers that appear unexpectedly, or primary cancers that are present but not detected, it is important to understand the characteristic imaging features of liver metastases from various primary malignancies. This is because treatment strategies can differ according to the given primary cancer. For example, a solitary metastasis from colorectal adenocarcinoma can be resected, while one from pancreatic adenocarcinoma needs to be treated with chemotherapy.

        Frequency of Liver Metastases

        Liver metastases have been frequently identified in patients who have secondary tumors from various primary cancers, mainly those from gastrointestinal organs, according to data from the SEER database (1) and knowledge of different histologic subtypes (Table 1). Data from another database limited to adenocarcinoma cases (5) have shown the liver to be one of the organs most affected by metastatic disease, which arises most frequently from primary sites in the colorectum, pancreas, stomach, breast, and lung (Table 2). More than 50%–90% of colorectal, breast, gastric, lung, and prostate cancers are adenocarcinomas (6). The second most frequently observed subtype is squamous cell carcinoma, which is found with several cancers (6). A large number of other malignant tumors from almost any site—including neuroendocrine tumor (NET), gastrointestinal stromal tumor (GIST), malignant melanoma, soft-tissue sarcoma, and lymphoma—can metastasize to the liver (4).

        Thus, a better understanding of the frequent sites of primary cancers could be useful in clinical cases involving more than one primary tumor. For example, in a patient with simultaneous colorectal and prostate carcinomas, multiple liver masses may likely be due to colorectal liver metastases, considering the frequency of these cancers (Table 1).

        Why the Liver Is a Dominant Site of Metastases

        Tumor cells are known to colonize in specific organs (organ-specific metastases) other than the liver. For example, 91% of ovarian cancers metastasize to the peritoneum (Table 2) (5), 90% of prostate cancers metastasize to bone, and 85% of renal cell carcinomas metastasize to the lung. Even so, various primary cancers preferably metastasize to the liver, and several factors are speculated to be related to organ-specific metastases of the liver (7).

        Blood Flow Factors (Portal Circulation)

        The liver’s vast blood supply, which originates from the portal and systemic systems, may provide a fertile environment for metastases. On the basis of pathologic examination of the anatomic structure of the vascular system and the accompanying hemodynamic flow, Ewing (8) pointed out the importance of the associated metastatic dissemination and geometric factors. The high frequency of liver metastases that results from pancreatic adenocarcinoma can be explained by Ewing’s mechanical theory.

        Factors That Create a Fertile Environment in the Liver Parenchyma

        The fenestra of the hepatic sinusoidal endothelium may facilitate formation of a preferable environment for metastases and penetration of malignant cells into the hepatic parenchyma (microenvironment) (9). From pathologic and clinical perspectives, Paget (10) postulated that metastases are not events due to chance; rather, they occur because some tumor cells (“seeds”) grow preferentially in the microenvironment of select organs (“soil”), and metastases occur only when the appropriate seed is implanted in suitable soil. The fact that uveal melanoma specifically has liver metastases, even when the primary site is far from the liver (11), may support the potential role of Paget’s seed-soil theory, but it cannot be explained by Ewing’s theory.

        Tumor-related Factors

        The theories of both Paget (10) and Ewing (8) could be partially correct, but neither theory alone can explain all of the phenomena associated with organ-specific metastases.

        Studies have shown that the primary tumor antecedently consolidates the microenvironment of the metastatic site (soil), for ease in colonization of tumor cells (seeds), by effusing organ-specific exosomes; this process constitutes formation of a premetastatic niche
        (12). Exosomes, which range from 40 to 160 nm in diameter (average, 100 nm), are a subset of extracellular vesicles that are surrounded by a lipid bilayer membrane and secreted by most eukaryotic cells (13). The exosomes play a crucial role in regulating tumor growth, metastasis, and angiogenesis during the process of cancer development (14). It has been shown that different types of proteins (integrins) are found on the surface of exosomes that are derived from different types of malignant cells (15). For example, in the liver, exosomes expressed by the specific integrins αv and β5 preferentially fuse with Kupffer cells (15). Thus, tumor exosomes derive organ-specific metastasis, during which there is integrin-mediated fusion with resident cells (16). As cellular changes to the target organ are induced after uptake of the tumor exosomes, the changes can promote proliferation and colonization of cancer cells at the metastatic sites (Fig 1).

        Metastasis Process

        Although the mechanisms that drive each step of the metastasis process remain relatively unclear owing to their complexity, recently introduced engineered models have elucidated key features of the metastatic cascade (17). There are six major steps involved in metastatic progression of tumors: (a) invasion of the basement membrane and cell migration, (b) intravasation within the surrounding vasculature or lymphatic system, (c) survival within the circulation, (d) extravasation from the vasculature to the secondary tissues, (e) subsequent proliferation at distant locations, and finally (f) colonization of secondary tumor sites (Fig 1) (18).

        One important cell remodeling process that occurs during the initial local invasion is epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (19). During this transition, there is fragmentation of the basement membrane along with loss of polarized organization of the epithelial cells, after which the cells become migratory, develop invasive capabilities, and then transform into highly migratory mesenchymal cells. During the reverse process—that is, mesenchymal-to-epithelial transition—this action appears to support a metastatic outgrowth for proliferation at distant sites (Fig 1) (19).

        Imaging Techniques

        Accurate detection and characterization of liver metastases depends on optimization of imaging modalities. Multiple imaging techniques including CT, MRI, and fluorine 18–fluorodeoxyglucose PET/CT are widely used. In a meta-analysis (20), MRI, CT, and PET/CT had sensitivity of 93.1% (95% CI: 88.4%, 96.0%), 82.1% (95% CI: 74.0%, 88.1%), and 74.1% (95% CI: 62.1%, 83.3%), respectively. It is important that the majority of the MRI procedures included in the analysis were carried out by using gadoxetic acid (Primovist; Bayer Schering) and included diffusion-weighted imaging.

        The hepatobiliary contrast agent gadoxetic acid, which has replaced previously used hepatospecific contrast agents such as mangafodipir trisodium and superparamagnetic iron oxide, has high sensitivity (90.3%) for detecting 1-cm or smaller liver metastases on hepatobiliary phase MR images (21). The diagnostic performance of diffusion-weighted imaging and hepatobiliary phase MRI and the efficacy of combining these sequences for evaluation of liver metastases have been demonstrated in previous studies (22,23).

        For example, the per-lesion sensitivity of gadoxetic acid–enhanced MRI combined with diffusion-weighted imaging (90%) was found to exceed that of other imaging modalities, such as CT and PET/CT (sensitivity of 68% and 61%, respectively), especially in detection of lesions smaller than 10 mm (sensitivity of 74% for MRI combined with diffusion-weighted imaging vs 16% and 9% for CT and PET/CT, respectively) (23). In the same report, it was shown that the diagnostic performance of PET/CT was relatively low for detection of small (<10 mm) lesions.

        Furthermore, diffusion can be quantified by using the apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC), with low ADC corresponding to restricted diffusion. The mean ADC of malignant lesions has been reported to be 0.980 × 10−3 mm2/sec, and applying an ADC cutoff of 1.066 × 10−3 mm2/sec has resulted in specificity of 86.6% and sensitivity of 73.6% for detection of malignancy (24). Although there may be an overlap in the ADC for liver metastases and other benign or malignant focal lesions (24,25), some metastases, such as those of NET and lymphoma, can have characteristically low ADCs (26,27), while others such as mucinous metastases can have higher ADCs (28).

        Other MRI sequences, including T1- and T2-weighted imaging with or without fat suppression, as well as CT, also are necessary to characterize focal liver lesions. Thus, CT and MRI findings are reviewed in this article. Gallium 68–tetraazacyclododecane tetraacetic acid-octreotate PET/CT is increasingly becoming an option for patients with known NET. PET appears to have good sensitivity and specificity (92.9% and 75%, respectively) for detecting liver metastases (29), although further studies are needed to determine whether it should be performed routinely.

        Vascular Supply of Liver Metastasis and Dynamic Contrast-enhanced Study

        For characterization, it is crucial to combine pre- and postcontrast CT and MR images for a dynamic contrast-enhanced imaging study. Solid liver metastases are typically supplied by arterial blood flow (30); hence, they can be classified as hypovascular or hypervascular depending on the degree of the underlying hepatic arterial supply (31). Hypovascular metastases show decreased enhancement relative to the background liver parenchyma and are most conspicuous on portal venous phase images, which are acquired approximately 60–80 seconds after initial injection of contrast agent (32).

        Hypervascular metastases enhance earlier than the background liver on arterial phase images, which are acquired approximately 30–40 seconds after the initial injection (32). The lesions show washout on portal phase images and similar or decreased enhancement relative to the background liver parenchyma on delayed phase images, which are acquired approximately 150–180 seconds after the initial injection (32). Thus, dynamic contrast-enhanced images can depict both hypovascular and hypervascular metastases, although the optimal number and choice of acquisition phases for CT remain contentious, given the potential risks associated with higher radiation doses.

        Importance of Noting Specific Imaging Features That Suggest a Specific Primary Cancer

        The imaging findings of liver metastases largely depend on various primary tumor–specific factors, such as histopathologic category (eg, adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, small cell carcinoma, transitional cell carcinoma, NET, sarcoma, melanoma, lymphoma), tumor differentiation (eg, well, moderate, poor), histologic behavior (eg, solid, cystic, mucinous), and intratumor alterations (eg, necrosis, degeneration, hemorrhage). However, the imaging features of several primary cancers sometimes overlap. For example, liver metastases from colorectal and gastric adenocarcinoma can have similar appearances, manifesting as multifocal hypovascular nodular lesions with hypoattenuation on noncontrast CT images, hypointensity on T1-weighted images, and moderate hyperintensity on T2-weighted images.

        Even with overlapping findings, it is not difficult to diagnose liver metastasis in a patient with a known sole primary cancer. However, a better understanding of the typical and characteristic imaging findings could aid in making a more precise diagnosis in the following situations: (a) Primary cancers of hollow organs such as gastrointestinal organs, the lungs, and the bladder, owing to the appearance of metastases that cannot be applied to the liver, which is a parenchymal organ. For such cases, it is important to know the typical findings of each primary cancer. (b) Unknown primary tumors. For example, hypovascular and hypervascular liver masses each would indicate a different primary cancer. (c) More than one primary tumor. For example, a hypovascular mass with calcification would indicate primary colorectal adenocarcinoma rather than primary prostatic adenocarcinoma in a patient with both of these primary cancers. (d) Another emergent malignancy. For example, in a patient with colorectal adenocarcinoma, multiple hypervascular masses might indicate the potential for another primary cancer such as a new NET. (e) Transformation to a different histopathologic subtype. In addition, some characteristic imaging findings represent biomarkers of prognosis or treatment response.

        Desmoplastic Reactions around the Tumor

        Desmoplastic reactions associated with malignant neoplasms can evoke a fibrotic response by invading healthy tissue (33). These reactions have been described in association with not only various primary tumors but also metastatic sites (34). Desmoplastic reactions around the tumor, which consist of fibrosis growth caused by cancer-associated fibroblasts around tumor cells (Figs 2, E1), account for part of the formation of the premetastatic niche and can promote local progression of metastases (Fig 1). These reactions also affect the imaging findings of the metastases and various primary tumor factors. In addition, the desmoplastic reactions produced by cancer-associated fibroblasts, as well as cancer-associated fibroblasts by themselves, might reduce the penetrance of chemotherapy drugs, acting as shields, and contribute to chemoresistance (35).

        Peripheral and Peritumor Enhancement

        Desmoplastic reactions around colorectal liver metastases are closely related to the peripheral and peritumor enhancement seen on arterial phase and portal venous phase images
        (Fig 3). Other histopathologic changes in the surrounding hepatic parenchyma, such as lymphocytic infiltration, compressed hepatic parenchyma, and sinusoidal congestion, also can be related to this enhancement (36).

        Histopathologically, colorectal liver metastases are classified into three distinct histopathologic growth patterns—desmoplastic, pushing, and replacement—which are based on the interface between the cancer cells of the metastasis and the cancer cells of the surrounding normal liver and different mechanisms to obtain a vascular supply (37). It is important that responses to chemotherapy are different, depending on the histopathologic growth patterns (38). The desmoplastic pattern corresponds to metastases with a prominent desmoplastic reaction, showing peripheral and peritumor enhancement. Thus, the imaging features might enable prediction of the response to chemotherapy, although there have been no studies linking imaging features to histologic subtypes.

        Distinct Arterioportal Shunts with Pancreatic Metastases

        Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma is the most common form of pancreatic cancer and is characterized by abundant fibrosis, which is caused by desmoplastic reaction around the tumor. Extremely dense desmoplastic reactions are equally observed at the metastatic site, with the fibrous stroma more frequently and excessively broad beyond the cancer cells (34).

        Extremely dense desmoplastic reactions tend to compress the peripheral vessels and reduce blood flow, and more hypotensive portal or hepatic veins are more easily affected. This phenomenon can be depicted as arterioportal shunts accompanied by metastatic nodules
        (Fig 4). If arterioportal shunts are observed in patients with pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, the existence of tiny liver metastases should be assumed.

        Pseudocirrhosis

        After chemotherapy, pseudocirrhosis, a rare condition, can lead to morphologic changes such as multiple liver metastases. This is seen particularly with liver metastases from breast cancer; however, it is rarely observed with liver metastases from pancreatic, colon, medullary thyroid, or esophageal cancers (39). It is difficult to predict the occurrence of pseudocirrhosis because it is not related to the number of metastases, the histopatholic features, or the chemotherapeutic regimen (40). Although the pathophysiologic mechanism of pseudocirrhosis has yet to be elucidated, it might be deeply involved with desmoplastic reaction. Therefore, abundant fibrosis caused by desmoplastic reaction around the tumor, fibrotic changes caused by chemotherapy, repeated tumor shrinkage and enlargement, and nodular regenerative hyperplasia in response to chemotherapy might invoke the irregular atrophy-hypertrophy complex of hepatic parenchyma (41).

        In addition, a relatively good response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy and a relatively long survival period could lead to morphometric changes in the liver (Fig 5). Although CT images depict hepatic morphometric changes that mimic cirrhosis, residual metastases cannot be separately represented by fibrous changes (41). While ADC-based quantification might facilitate accurate assessment of the hepatic condition by means of discrimination between residual metastases and fibrous change (42), there have been few descriptions of MRI findings.

        Characteristic Imaging Findings

        In diagnosis of liver metastases, several characteristic imaging findings need to be taken into consideration. These findings include tumor vascularity, the characteristic atttenuation values and signal intensities of lesions due to specific primary cancers, specific signs due to characteristic pathologic entities, the pathognomonic distribution, and rare intraductal metastatic forms (Tables 3, 4). Along with accurate detection of the lesion, consideration of the following issues is necessary: whether the imaging findings are consistent with the known primary cancer, the possibility of another emergent malignancy, and the possibility of transformation into a different histopathologic subtype.

        Target Sign on T2-weighted Images of Hypovascular Metastases

        Metastatic adenocarcinomas are the most common type of carcinoma (6) and are often accompanied by various degrees of fibrosis, liquefactive necrosis, and degeneration in predominantly central areas (43). At CT, metastatic adenocarcinomas appear as nonspecific hypovascular lesions with peripheral and peritumor enhancement on arterial or portal venous phase images (Fig 3) (36). In contrast, at MRI, in predominantly central areas, fibrous change or old hemorrhage is depicted as hypointensity on T1- and T2-weighted images, with liquefactive necrosis or degeneration depicted as clear areas of hyperintensity on T2-weighted images and slight hyperintensity on T1-weighted images (44,45).

        However, peripherally dominant viable tumor cells are depicted as moderate-hyperintensity lesions on T2-weighted images and hypointense lesions on T1-weighted images (Figs 6, 7, E2A). The histopathologic contrast between central and peripheral areas is seen as the target sign on T2-weighted images (Figs 6B, 7A, E2A) (44). This finding has been observed in 25% of metastases, those from colorectal adenocarcinoma in particular (46).

        Peripheral Rim Washout Sign

        The peripheral washout sign is defined as an enhancing liver lesion with a peripheral rim of decreased enhancement relative to its center and the surrounding liver parenchyma on delayed phase MR images obtained by using extracellular contrast agents (Fig E3) (47). The washout sign is observable in 25% of patients with liver metastases from NET or from colorectal, breast, or gastric carcinomas (47). Peripheral washout may reflect a transitory perfusion within the well-vascularized peripheral portion of the lesion, which has both good arterial supply and good venous withdrawal of contrast material (47).

        Target Sign on Hepatobiliary Phase Images of Hypovascular Metastases

        Metastases are usually hypointense on hepatobiliary phase images because of their lack of normal hepatocytes. However,

        metastatic adenocarcinoma with fibrous stroma occasionally may demonstrate central areas of relative hyperintensity but typically has lower signal intensity compared with the background parenchyma; this is described as the target sign, which is observed in 47%–70% of metastatic adenocarcinomas
        (Figs 7C, E2B) (48,49). While either increased uptake due to retention in fibrotic stromata or aberrant expression of organic anion–transporting polypeptide 1B3 has been suggested as the mechanism underlying this finding, this remains a topic of debate (50). The target sign of metastases of breast cancer may be associated with a poor response to chemotherapy (51).

        Peritumor Hyperintensity on Hepatobiliary Phase Images

        With some liver metastases, such as from NET, GIST, or colorectal adenocarcinomas (Fig E4) (49), peritumor hyperintensity, which is a homogeneously hyperintense rim surrounding the tumor, can be observed on hepatobiliary phase images, although the frequency of this finding has not been established. Histopathologically, peritumor hyperintensity corresponds to peritumor hyperplasia, which is defined as a rim of hyperplastic hepatocytes surrounding the tumor (52).

        The pathogenesis of peritumor hyperplasia remains controversial, but two mechanisms should be considered. One possibility is that a perfusion abnormality due to tumor vascular invasion causes increased arterial blood flow in the peritumor liver parenchyma and consequent hepatocyte hyperplasia. Another possibility is that regenerative changes occur in hepatocytes that are compressed by tumors with expansive growth (49). A relationship between degree of peritumor hyperintensity and progression-free survival has been observed with liver metastases from NET (53).

        Hypervascular Metastases

        Hypervascular metastases typically arise from hypervascular primary tumors, including NET, renal cell carcinoma, thyroid carcinoma, choriocarcinoma, pheochromocytoma, malignant melanoma, and sarcomas (54), which show hypoattenuation on nonenhanced CT images, moderate hypointensity on T1-weighted images, and moderate hyperintensity on T2-weighted images. Dynamic contrast-enhanced CT and MRI depict early enhancement on arterial phase images and washout on portal venous phase images (Fig 8) (32), which are indistinguishable from the enhancement findings of hepatocellular cancer (Fig E5).

        However, multifocal hypervascular lesions in patients without cirrhosis might be more likely to be liver metastases (55). In addition, liver metastases from gastrointestinal or breast adenocarcinoma potentially can have an appearance similar to that of a hypervascular lesion owing to marked peripheral and peritumor enhancement (Fig E6) (36,54).

        The washout in small (≤5 mm) metastases from NET on portal venous phase images is sometimes unclear, although arterial phase hyperenhancement can be observed (56). In addition, hypervascular metastases frequently accompany various degrees of degeneration (Fig 9) (54,57).

        Cystic Appearance on T2-weighted Images

        Cystic appearances include the following three entities: cystic changes due to necrosis or degeneration, cystic metastases due to the cystic components of the primary tumor, and marked hyperintensity on T2-weighted images due to the primary histologic features. These three entities have the potential to appear as the same finding, showing marked hyperintensity at T2-weighted MRI, and can be indistinguishable from each other. Thus, these three conditions have sometimes been comprehensively referred to as cystic liver metastases (57–59).

        Severe necrosis or degeneration tends to appear in metastases from NET, GIST, sarcoma, melanoma, angiosarcoma, testicular carcinoma, or squamous cell carcinoma from any site (54,57). Rapid growth of hypervascular metastases beyond the blood supply sometimes results in severe degeneration or necrosis and is depicted as characteristic areas of clear hyperintensity on T2-weighted images and as hypoattenuating areas on CT images (Fig 9). The wall and inner surface of the lesions show ragged, serrated, and ill-defined enhancement due to residual viable tumor cells with fibrotic tissues (Fig 10). This irregular enhancement usually can be distinguised from the appearance of a simple hepatic cyst. However, complete necrosis or degeneration of metastases can sometimes have an appearance similar to that of a simple hepatic cyst (Fig E7).

        When the primary tumor—for example, intraductal papillary mucinous carcinoma of the pancreas, adenoid cystic carcinoma, or ovarian serous and mucinous carcinoma—consists of cystic components (58), the liver metastases also can be composed of cystic components. These components appear as a multilocular cystic pattern with enhancing septa and irregular thickened walls (Fig E8). In the case of unilocular cystic metastases with limited enhancing walls, the appearance is similar to that of simple hepatic cysts, which exhibit hypoattenuation on noncontrast CT images, clear hyperintensity on T2-weighted images, and hypointensity on T1-weighted images, accompanied by no enhancement (Fig 11).

        Liver metastases of mucinous adenocarcinoma or uterine endometrial carcinoma show marked hyperintensity on T2-weighted images owing to abundant mucin or endometrial components, despite lack of necrosis, lack of degeneration, and lack of cystic components (59). These markedly hyperintense metastases seen at T2-weighted imaging exhibit thick irregularly enhancing walls accompanying internal septa and mural nodules that result from scattering tumor cells (Fig 12).

        Intratumor Hemorrhage (Fluid-Fluid Level)

        Intratumor hemorrhage (fluid-fluid level) can be caused by the hemorrhagic content, which also is closely related to necrosis or degeneration associated with metastases. This is encountered especially in metastases from NET and is also found in tumors such as melanoma, angiosarcoma, ovarian carcinoma, leiomyosarcoma, and squamous cell carcinoma (60,61). Intratumor hemorrhage can exhibit slight hyperintensity on T1-weighted images (45) and characteristic fluid-fluid levels on T2-weighted images (Fig 13) (60). The attenuation and signal intensity at imaging depend on the degree and duration of the intratumor hemorrhage. Intratumor hemorrhage infrequently induces hepatic rupture and hemoperitoneum (61).

        Marked Hyperintensity on T1-weighted Images

        Hyperintensity on T1-weighted images suggests the presence of paramagnetic substances, including melanin (with malignant melanoma), extracellular methemoglobin (with hemorrhage), protein (with ovarian adenocarcinoma, multiple myeloma, and pancreatic mucinous cystic tumors), and necrosis (with colorectal adenocarcinoma). In fact, marked hyperintensity at T1-weighted imaging is characteristic of malignant melanoma (Fig 14A) (45). Other lesions can show mild to slight hyperintensity on T1-weighted images (Fig 6C) (45). Note that marked hyperintensity at T1-weighted imaging can mask the vascularity seen on dynamic contrast-enhanced MR images (Fig 14B). Melanin quantification at T1-weighted imaging is significantly associated with overall survival (62).

        Calcification

        Calcification related to liver metastases is most commonly observed in primary tumors of mucinous adenocarcinoma of the colorectum and stomach, breast, and high-grade serous carcinoma, although it is rarely observed in other tumors such as thyroid carcinoma and chondrosarcoma (63). Intratumor calcification is clearly depicted on noncontrast CT images (Fig 15), with patterns and distributions (central and peripheral) that have been found to vary (64).

        In addition, the calcification is often accompanied by capsular retraction adjacent to the subcapsular area (Fig 15C). In small metastases, it is possible that only the calcification will be depicted at CT (Fig 15A); therefore, care should be taken not to miss the small area of high attenuation.

        Although the development of calcifications is considered to be a marker of treatment response, especially in KRAS wild-type colorectal adenocarcinoma treated with antiepidermal growth factor receptor drugs, the correlation between calcification of pretreatment metastases and effectiveness of chemotherapies or prognosis remains controversial (65).

        Hepatic Capsular Retraction

        When the convex hepatic contour exhibits focal flattening or concavity, this is defined as hepatic capsular retraction (66). A variety of hepatic neoplasms and nonneoplastic lesions, including liver metastases, have been reported to be associated with hepatic capsular retraction. Capsular retraction usually occurs adjacent to subcapsular metastases (Figs 6, 15, E2) from primary tumors such as lung, breast, and colorectal adenocarcinomas (66). These tumors tend to appear to accompany substantial fibrosis caused by desmoplastic reactions around the metastases (34).

        Vessel-penetrating Sign

        The vessel-penetrating sign is an imaging finding in which intrahepatic vessels course through the tumor without disruption or constriction, and it is often observed in primary and secondary lymphomas (Fig E9) (67). Primary hepatic lymphoma is extremely rare, whereas secondary liver involvement occurs in 50% of patients with non-Hodgkin lymphoma (67,68). In addition to the vessel-penetrating sign, other typical imaging features—such as homogeneous attenuation or signal intensity at CT or MRI, respectively; homogeneous poor enhancement relative to the background liver parenchyma on contrast-enhanced images; and relatively low ADC—could be useful clues for diagnosing lymphoma (27).

        When a multifocal liver mass is encountered without the vessel-penetrating sign at follow-up of a patient with lymphoma, it could suggest another newly appearing malignant tumor with liver metastases (Fig 16). However, this is a rare occurrence.

        Distribution of Liver Metastases

        The most common distribution of liver metastases is that of multifocal nodular lesions. However, these metastases sometimes manifest as a solitary mass or confluent masses (Fig E10). In addition, the following two characteristic distributions should be known: diffuse small nodular metastases and metastases prominently located in the gallbladder fossa of the liver.

        Diffuse Small Nodular Metastases (Miliary Liver Metastases)

        On rare occasions, tumors can infiltrate diffusely and spread as tiny nodules in the whole liver. This phenomenon is referred to as miliary liver metastases and has been reported with lung and breast carcinomas, uveal melanoma, and NET (69). This manifestation can sometimes be accompanied by diffuse intrasinusoidal metastases, which are clearly depicted at MRI as numerous tiny nodular metastases on T2- or diffusion-weighted images (Fig 17). On the other hand, CT images do not depict these tiny metastatic nodules and show only enlargement of the entire liver (Fig 17B).

        Metastases Prominently Located in the Gallbladder Fossa

        Gallbladder tumors often metastasize prominently to the gallbladder fossa of the liver as multiple or solitary lesions (Fig 18). This occurs because the drainage veins of the gallbladder (cystic veins) flow into the intrahepatic portal branches or sinusoids around the gallbladder fossa (70).

        Rare Intraductal Forms of Metastases

        Intrasinusoidal Metastases

        Most metastases form nodular lesions in the liver parenchyma and rarely appear after ductal metastases. Rarely, tumors infiltrate and spread along the hepatic sinusoids instead of forming discrete masses, making the disease radiologically occult. These rare metastases can be associated with solid tumors, including breast cancer, small cell lung cancer, gastric cancer, and melanoma, and may cause acute liver failure at a high rate (71).

        Despite these life-threatening conditions, noncontrast and contrast-enhanced CT and MRI sometimes depict hepatomegaly only (Fig 19). Hepatobiliary phase images can show diffuse hypointensity, which indicates no uptake of gadoxetic acid (Fig 19C). This is because expression of organic anion–transporting polypeptide 1B3 at the sinusoidal membrane, which uptakes gadoxetic acid, would be disturbed by the intrasinusoidal spread of malignant cells (72).

        Portal Vein Tumor Thrombosis

        In contrast to the thrombus seen with hepatocellular carcinoma, intrahepatic tumor thrombus occurs infrequently with liver metastases. Involvement of the intrahepatic portal vein has been reported with several tumors, such as gastric and colorectal adenocarcinoma and gastric NET (73). In general, intrahepatic tumor thrombus is usually associated with and shows continuity with nodular metastases (Fig 20).

        Biliary Metastasis

        There are two types of biliary metastases: biliary epithelium metastases and intraluminal metastases of the bile duct, and both types can occur concurrently. Intra- and extrabiliary epithelium metastases rarely occur with colorectal, gastric, breast, uterine, or ovarian carcinomas (74) and involve homogeneous thickening and enhancement of the bile duct, with smooth tapered dilatation of this duct.

        Extrabiliary epithelium metastases are typically accompanied by other metastases such as those involving a hepatic parenchymal mass or multiple lymph node metastases (Fig 21). When only the extrahepatic bile duct is involved, it is difficult to differentiate primary bile duct cancer. Furthermore, dilatation of the bile duct can sometimes be indistinguishable from compression of the bile duct by lymph node metastases within the hepatoduodenal ligament.

        Intraluminal metastases of the bile duct (ie, intrabiliary metastases) form a polypoid mass within the lumen of the bile duct. The polypoid mass can invade the biliary epithelium and lead to dilatation of the peripheral bile duct, which is often accompanied by a solid metastatic mass in the parenchyma (75). Such metastases have also been reported with colorectal adenocarcinoma, melanoma, and hepatocellular carcinoma (75).

        Intrabiliary growth metastases can be difficult to distinguish from cholangiocarcinoma; however, a periductal expansile mass has been found to be more common in intrabiliary metastases, whereas a periductal infiltrative lesion has been observed with cholangiocarcinoma (75). Furthermore, intrabiliary metastases can sometimes be indistinguishable from bile duct compression by liver metastases. However, it is more important that dilatation of the bile duct at the periphery of a liver metastasis could be an indicator of poor prognosis (76,77) (Fig E11).

        Lymphatic Invasion

        Intrahepatic lymphatic invasion by tumor cells is a manifestation of diffuse lymphatic spread of malignant cells. It has been reported in 4% of cases of liver metastases, especially those of gastric cancer and lymphoma (78). Malignant cells within lymphatic vessels form a periportal mass and dilate the lymphatic vessels.

        On CT images, a periportal soft-tissue mass is depicted as a hypoattenuating lesion with attenuation similar to that of water; on T2-weighted images, it is seen as a lesion with moderate hyperintensity. In contrast, dilated lymphatic vessels show periportal hypoattenuation at CT and periportal hyperintensity at T2-weighted (Fig 22) and diffusion-weighted imaging.

        Transformation of Primary Tumor into a Different Histopathologic Type

        Transformation of a primary cancer into a different histopathologic tumor type has been reported as a resistance process in response to targeted therapies; this is closely related to the earlier-mentioned epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (19,79). For example, histopathologic transformation into small cell lung carcinoma can occur in 3%–15% of patients with non–small cell lung carcinoma, mainly in the setting of adenocarcinoma. In addition, this transformation has been described as a cause of acquired tyrosine kinase inhibitor resistance (80). Treatment-related neuroendocrine prostate cancer transdifferentiates from adenocarcinoma as an adaptive response to androgen receptor pathway inhibition (Fig 23) (81).

        Although transformation into aggressive lymphomas such as diffuse large B-cell lymphoma or Burkitt lymphoma also is known to occur with indolent lymphomas such as follicular lymphoma, marginal zone lymphoma, and lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma, it has been best described as occurring with follicular lymphoma (82).

        Transformation of a primary cancer into a different histopathologic type can also affect the imaging findings, reflecting histologic features, with the liver metastasis also exhibiting an appearance similar to that of the primary change
        .

        Imaging Biomarkers of Prognosis or Treatment Response

        Several imaging findings described in earlier sections of this review could be used for predicting the prognosis or the effectiveness of chemotherapies, serving as imaging biomarkers. However, it is necessary to understand that these results are not absolute and could change in the future on the basis of data from larger studies.

        Conclusion

        Although the imaging features of liver metastases from various primary malignancies overlap, obtaining a better understanding of the mechanisms that underlie organ-specific metastases and determining the characteristic features of liver metastases from specific primary cancers can help provide a more precise diagnosis.

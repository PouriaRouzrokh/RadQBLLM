        Abstract

        Heterotopic pancreas is a congenital anomaly in which pancreatic tissue is anatomically separate from the main gland. The most common locations of this displacement include the upper gastrointestinal tract—specifically, the stomach, duodenum, and proximal jejunum. Less common sites are the esophagus, ileum, Meckel diverticulum, biliary tree, mesentery, and spleen. Uncomplicated heterotopic pancreas is typically asymptomatic, with the lesion being discovered incidentally during an unrelated surgery, during an imaging examination, or at autopsy. The most common computed tomographic appearance of heterotopic pancreas is that of a small oval intramural mass with microlobulated margins and an endoluminal growth pattern. The attenuation and enhancement characteristics of these lesions parallel their histologic composition. Acinus-dominant lesions demonstrate avid homogeneous enhancement after intravenous contrast material administration, whereas duct-dominant lesions are hypovascular and heterogeneous. At magnetic resonance imaging, the heterotopic pancreas is isointense to the orthotopic pancreas, with characteristic T1 hyperintensity and early avid enhancement after intravenous gadolinium-based contrast material administration. Heterotopic pancreatic tissue has a rudimentary ductal system in which an orifice is sometimes visible at imaging as a central umbilication of the lesion. Complications of heterotopic pancreas include pancreatitis, pseudocyst formation, malignant degeneration, gastrointestinal bleeding, bowel obstruction, and intussusception. Certain complications may be erroneously diagnosed as malignancy. Paraduodenal pancreatitis is thought to be due to cystic degeneration of heterotopic pancreatic tissue in the medial wall of the duodenum. Recognizing the characteristic imaging features of heterotopic pancreas aids in differentiating it from cancer and thus in avoiding unnecessary surgery.

        Introduction

        Heterotopic pancreas is a congenital anomaly in which pancreatic tissue is anatomically separate from the main gland and without vascular or ductal continuity. It is variably referred to as ectopic, aberrant, or accessory pancreas, as well as pancreatic choristoma and adenomyoma. The true incidence of heterotopic pancreas is difficult to determine, as most affected patients are asymptomatic. Autopsy results indicate a prevalence of 0.5%–13.7%, and the condition is estimated to be incidentally encountered during 0.2% of upper abdominal surgeries and 0.9% of gastrectomies (1,2).

        The most common location for heterotopic pancreas is the upper gastrointestinal tract—specifically, the stomach, duodenum, and proximal jejunum. Less common sites are the esophagus, ileum, Meckel diverticulum, and biliary tree.
        Rarely, pancreatic tissue has been reported in more distant sites such as the mediastinum, lungs, spleen, fallopian tubes, umbilicus, and omentum (1).

        Uncomplicated heterotopic pancreas is typically asymptomatic, with the lesion being discovered incidentally during unrelated surgery, during imaging examination, or at autopsy. Although the rarity of this entity precludes large-series investigations, some study investigators have reported symptoms in 30%–50% of patients in their small cohorts (2). Epigastric pain is the most common symptom, followed by chronic abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and rarely, weight loss. Male preponderance is seen among adults, with the incidence of disease peaking during the 4th, 5th, and 6th decades of life (2,3).

        In this article, the authors review theories regarding the development of heterotopic pancreas, histopathologic features of heterotopic pancreas, and how they correlate with the imaging findings. The authors also describe the most common sites of involvement, imaging features, and most common complications of this anomaly—paraduodenal pancreatitis in particular. It is hoped that increased awareness of the imaging appearances of heterotopic pancreas and the associated complications will aid in preoperative diagnosis and decrease the morbidity associated with erroneous diagnoses.

        Embryologic Features

        Congenital anomalies are divided into three categories based on the complex embryology of the pancreas: fusion anomalies, migration anomalies, and duplication anomalies (4). Heterotopic pancreas falls into the category of migration anomalies. The embryologic basis of heterotopic pancreas is unknown, but the most widely held theory is the misplacement theory. According to the misplacement theory, deposits of pancreatic tissue are “dropped” into the developing gastrointestinal system, in anatomic isolation from the main body of the pancreas (5,6). This theory accounts for the fact that in the majority of cases, heterotopic pancreatic tissue is found in other derivatives of the primitive foregut. Two other theories regarding the pathogenesis of heterotopic pancreas are the metaplasia theory and the totipotent cell theory (7). According to the metaplasia theory, endodermal tissues migrate to the submucosa during embryogenesis and transform into pancreatic tissue. According to the totipotent cell theory, however, endodermal cells lining the gut differentiate into pancreatic tissue. Genetic mutations in association with heterotopic pancreas have been found in animals that have either an absence of the genes necessary for pancreatic embryogenesis and development of the foregut (8,9) or defects in the signaling that is necessary for these processes.

        Histopathologic Features

        Heterotopic pancreas essentially mimics the orthotopic pancreas at gross and histologic specimen evaluations. Gross specimens show a firm intramural mass that has a lobular shape and a well-defined interface with surrounding tissues (10). Most lesions (80%) are solitary and smaller than 3 cm, but they can range in size from 0.2 to 5.0 cm (10,11). A heterotopic pancreatic lesion in the gastrointestinal tract is categorized as subepithelial, which is defined as a mass covered with normal mucosa (12). Heterotopic pancreas is found in the submucosal layer of the bowel wall in 54% of cases, spans the submucosa and muscularis propria in 23% of cases, is located in the muscularis propria in 8% of cases, is located in the subserosa in 11% of cases, and involves the entire wall in 4% of cases (13).

        Although first reported in 1727 by Schultz, heterotopic pancreas was not histologically confirmed until 1859 when it was described by Klob (14). Three types of heterotopic pancreas were described in the first histologic classification system, as described by Heinrich in 1909 (Table 1) (14). The first and most common type of heterotopic pancreatic tissue is composed of all the elements of the normal pancreas, including acini, ducts, and islet cells. The second and third histologic types are dominated by either acini or ducts. This classification system was further modified in 1973 by Fuentes to include a fourth histologic type of heterotopic pancreatic tissue, which is composed of only islet cells (Table 1) (14).

        Radiologic-Pathologic Correlation

        Multiple studies indicate that the contrast enhancement characteristics and homogeneity of heterotopic pancreas correlate with its histologic composition
        (1,5,10,12,15,16). Lesions exhibiting enhancement greater than or similar to that of the orthotopic pancreas are dominated by acini, whereas lesions with less enhancement are dominated by ducts and hypertrophied muscle. Similarly, lesions that are homogeneous on computed tomographic (CT) images demonstrate acinar predominance microscopically, whereas heterogeneous lesions show ductal predominance. The ill-defined lesion margins seen on CT images correspond to the lobular morphology of the acinar component seen in histologic specimens. (15). Microscopically, the ductal system of heterotopic pancreatic tissue communicates with the bowel lumen (5), which may be difficult to visualize at imaging. The draining orifice of the rudimentary ductal system may be seen as central umbilication of the lesion grossly, endoscopically, or at imaging. When visualized, this central niche varies in size, with a diameter of 1–5 mm and a depth of 5–10 mm (17).

        Sites of Heterotopic Pancreas

        Gastrointestinal Tract

        Heterotopic pancreas is most commonly found in the proximal gastrointestinal tract. The most frequent locations are the stomach (25.5%), duodenum (27.7%), proximal jejunum (15.9%), Meckel diverticulum (5.3%), and ileum (2.8%) (2). Involvement of the esophagus is rare, with only 14 cases having been reported in the English literature (13,18).

        Although heterotopic pancreas is usually asymptomatic and incidentally discovered, depending on its location, this anomaly can cause symptoms. Esophageal lesions usually cause dysphagia and epigastric pain, occasionally cause gastroesophageal reflux and nausea, and rarely cause hematemesis (13). A mass at the pylorus may cause gastric obstruction. Jejunal heterotopic pancreas can serve as a lead point for intussusception. A lesion at the ampulla of Vater can result in biliary obstruction (1). Secondary inflammation of the overlying mucosa may cause clinically occult or symptomatic gastritis and rarely, ulceration (15,19).

        The most common location of ectopic pancreatic tissue in the stomach is along the greater curvature of the antrum, within 6 cm of the pylorus (15). Duodenal lesions are most often found in the descending part of the duodenum, and jejunal lesions manifest near the Treitz ligament (5). Most esophageal lesions are located in the distal third of the esophagus (13).

        On barium studies, heterotopic pancreas has the typical appearance of an extramucosal intramural tumor, with a broad base and smooth surface (Fig 1) (10,17). A central barium-filled pit, or umbilication, in the lesion is diagnostic of heterotopic pancreas and helps to differentiate it from other types of intramural masses. In one study (17), 20% of lesions had the typical small central umbilication. Rarely, this central depression is large and mimics a gastric ulcer or ulcerating submucosal tumor (17).

        At endoscopy, heterotopic pancreas appears as a submucosal mass with an endoluminal growth pattern (Fig 2) (2). The central umbilication is more often seen at endoscopy than at imaging (2). Owing to the subepithelial location of this lesion, superficial endoscopic biopsy results are nondiagnostic (19). Endoscopic US enables one to delineate the morphology and location of the lesion in the bowel wall and perform fine-needle aspiration biopsy. The most common appearance of heterotopic pancreas at endoscopic US is that of a solid submucosal mass that is hypoechoic relative to the hyperechoic submucosa and isoechoic relative to the muscularis propria (Fig 2) (1,20).

        CT images show an intramural oval mass with ill-defined or microlobulated margins (5). Heterotopic pancreatic lesions are usually small (<3 cm) and exhibit an endoluminal growth pattern (Fig 3) (2). The attenuation and enhancement characteristics of the lesion reflect its microscopic composition (2,5,10,12,15). Heterotopic tissue that is predominantly composed of acini shows homogeneous avid enhancement that is greater than or equal to that of the orthotopic pancreas. Hypoenhancing lesions are dominated by ductal structures and sometimes surrounded by hypertrophied muscle.

        The CT features that help to differentiate gastric and duodenal heterotopic pancreatic lesions from other more common submucosal masses are oval shape or flat surface, endoluminal growth pattern, antral gastric location, and ill-defined microlobulated margins
        (15). Lesions in the stomach are flat or ovoid and have a long diameter–to–short diameter ratio higher than 1.4 (Fig 4) (15). Duodenal and jejunal heterotopic pancreatic lesions are occasionally round—possibly because of differences between the wall tension in the duodenum and jejunum and the wall tension in the stomach (2,5). Gastric heterotopic pancreas typically has an antral location and an endoluminal growth pattern. These lesions grow toward the bowel lumen without deforming the external contour, as opposed to an exoluminal growth pattern where lesions grow exophytically from the bowel wall without bulging into the lumen. Gastrointestinal stromal tumors, the most common submucosal gastric tumors, manifest most often in the fundus or body of the stomach, and the majority of these neoplasms show exoluminal growth (15). Gastrointestinal stromal tumors can show a mixed growth pattern involving both luminal and external contour abnormalities that cause a dumbbell shape. This pattern is not seen with heterotopic pancreas.

        Heterotopic pancreatic lesions in the jejunum and those in the stomach and duodenum have many of the same morphologic and enhancement characteristics, with a few exceptions: although central umbilication of the lesion and hyperenhancement of the overlying mucosa are occasionally observed with gastric and duodenal lesions, these findings have not been reported with jejunal heterotopic pancreas (5). In addition, no dominant growth pattern has been observed in the jejunum, with lesions demonstrating endoluminal, exoluminal, and mixed growth patterns equally (Fig 5) (5). In one series (5) involving cases of jejunal heterotopic pancreas, a ductlike structure was retrospectively identified at CT in three (19%) of 16 patients; this structure was not readily identified in studies of the CT features of gastric lesions (5,10).

        The magnetic resonance (MR) imaging appearance of heterotopic pancreas mimics that of the orthotopic pancreas (1,21). The characteristic high signal intensity of the pancreas at T1-weighted MR imaging is particularly helpful for differentiating heterotopic pancreas from other lesions
        (1). The enhancement of the ectopic tissue parallels that of the native pancreas, with avid enhancement during the late arterial phase. Uncomplicated heterotopic pancreas is isointense to the orthotopic pancreas with all MR imaging sequences (Fig 6) (21). MR cholangiopancreatography can be helpful for identifying the rudimentary ductal system, a finding that is particularly useful for differentiating heterotopic pancreatic lesions from other submucosal masses. T2-weighted MR images and MR cholangiopancreatographic images are best for identifying a dilated duct in the heterotopic pancreas, which is often referred to as the “ectopic duct” sign (1).

        Heterotopic pancreas has been found in the ileum, colon, and Meckel diverticulum. The Meckel diverticulum may contain heterotopic pancreatic tissue in 5%–16% of cases (22). If located at the tip of the diverticulum, the heterotopic tissue may act as a lead point for inversion of the diverticulum into the ileum followed by intussusception (22). The enhancement, attenuation (at CT), and signal intensity (at MR imaging) characteristics of the lesion are similar to those of heterotopic pancreatic tissue seen elsewhere in the gastrointestinal tract. Once the diverticulum is inverted into the ileum, it has central fat surrounded by soft tissue, and with intussusception, there are multiple alternating concentric rings of fat and soft tissue (22).

        Mesentery

        Involvement of the jejunal mesentery is described in case reports and small series of mesenteric heterotopic pancreas. As with heterotopic pancreas in other sites, that involving the jejunal mesentery has morphologic and enhancement characteristics similar to those of the orthotopic pancreas. Mesenteric heterotopic pancreas manifests as an elongated lobulated lesion that has a broad base intimately associated with the jejunum (Fig 7). The long diameter–to–short diameter ratio of 3.0 in mesenteric lesions is substantially higher than the ratio of 1.4–1.5 of lesions in the stomach and proximal small bowel (23). The heterotopic tissue tapers as it extends into the mesentery. A ductlike structure paralleling the long axis of mesenteric lesions is seen more frequently than with pancreatic heterotopia in other locations (23). Microscopic evaluation reveals the pancreatic tissue infiltrating the jejunal wall and extending into the muscularis and submucosal layers (6). This histologic finding suggests this mesenteric variant may represent the exoluminal growth pattern of jejunal heterotopic pancreas.

        Hepatobiliary System

        Heterotopic pancreas may be present in the liver, biliary tree, and/or gallbladder. Approximately 29 cases have been reported in the gallbladder, comprising 1% of all heterotopic pancreas (24,25). Biliary lesions are even less common, with seven reported cases of heterotopic pancreas in the common bile duct and eight reported cases of intrahepatic heterotopic pancreas (26,27). Middle-aged women are the most commonly affected and rarely have symptoms (24). Rarely, the heterotopic tissue may cause obstruction that results in gallbladder hydrops, cholecystitis, gallbladder perforation, or biliary duct dilatation (26).

        Gross specimen analysis reveals focal thickening or nodularity of the gallbladder wall, with yellow firm irregular intramural nodules (25,28). Seventy-three percent of heterotopic pancreatic lesions of the gallbladder are submucosal, 17% are intramuscular, and 10% are subserosal. These lesions are 4–20 mm in size (mean size, 11 mm), and half of them are located in the neck of the gallbladder (25,27). In one case (27), the bile was evaluated and found to contain elevated levels of amylase and lipase. Pancreatic heterotopia in the bile duct is submucosal and may cause a focal stricture or intraluminal polypoid lesion. Heterotopic pancreatic tissue along the intrahepatic bile ducts associated with biliary strictures and hepatolithiasis has been reported (29).

        It is difficult to diagnose hepatobiliary heterotopic pancreas preoperatively, and the diagnosis is usually not rendered prospectively. US images show an echogenic, immobile, nonshadowing polypoid mass in the neck of the gallbladder or near the cystic duct (28). There may be secondary evidence of cystic duct obstruction with hydrops or cholecystitis. Bile duct heterotopic pancreas has intermediate echogenicity and appears as an immobile intraluminal filling defect at cholangiography (28).

        Spleen

        Heterotopic pancreatic tissue in the spleen is exceedingly rare, accounting for 0.4%–1.0% of heterotopic pancreas cases (30). Lesions are typically small and subcapsular (Fig 8) (31). In a single reported case of uncomplicated pancreatic heterotopia in the spleen evaluated at MR imaging, the heterotopic tissue was found to have signal intensity and enhancement characteristics comparable to those of the orthotopic pancreas (31). Nearly all reported cases of splenic heterotopic pancreas have been complicated by neoplasms, including mucinous cystadenoma in two cases, mucinous cystadenocarcinoma in four cases, and insulinoma with lymph node metastasis in one case (32). In our experience, mucinous tumors that occur in splenic heterotopic pancreatic tissue are similar to those that occur in the native pancreas and manifest as complex masses that contain cystic mucin-filled compartments and solid components (Fig 9). Mucinous cystic splenic tumors may also arise from ectopic intestinal and ovarian tissue or invaginated capsular epithelium (30).

        Other Sites

        Other, rarely reported sites of heterotopic pancreas are the mediastinum, lungs, fallopian tubes, umbilicus, and omentum (33). There are 20 reported cases of mediastinal pancreatic heterotopia (34,35). Most of these cases are those involving young adults who presented with nonspecific symptoms, most commonly chest pain (34,35). There is a slight female predominance (35). All reported cases developed in the anterior mediastinum and were relatively large (3.5–16.0-cm) masses (34). The majority of mediastinal cases were cystic masses, with pancreatic tissue either in the cyst wall or within small mural nodules. However, a single case of a solid mass has been reported (34). There are few reported cases of pancreatic heterotopia in the lungs (36). Some of these cases were associated with enteric duplication or esophageal heterotopic tissue. Umbilical lesions mainly occur in the pediatric population, with half of the 14 reported cases diagnosed in patients younger than 1 year and only three reported cases involving patients older than 2 years. Pancreatic tissue in the umbilicus is thought to result from the differentiation of totipotent endodermal cells lining an omphalomesenteric duct remnant (7).

        Complications

        Heterotopic pancreatic tissue is susceptible to the same pathologic conditions that can affect the orthotopic pancreas, including pancreatitis, pseudocyst formation, and benign and malignant neoplasms. Additional complications specific to heterotopic pancreas include gastrointestinal bleeding, bowel obstruction, and intussusception.

        Acute pancreatitis is usually not evident at clinical or imaging examination; rather, it is discernable at microscopic analysis (20). When pancreatic enzyme levels are elevated, the elevation is mild owing to the small volume of inflamed tissue (20). The imaging findings are nonspecific and include thickening of the involved bowel wall and surrounding inflammation (Fig 10) (1,3,20). The heterotopic pancreatic tissue may enhance less avidly when it is acutely inflamed (3). Pseudocysts can form in the heterotopic tissue—either secondary to pancreatitis or as a result of duct obstruction and the accumulation of pancreatic juices (Fig 11).

        Malignant transformation of heterotopic pancreas is rare, occurring in 0.7%–1.8% of cases (37). The majority of reported cases have involved ductal adenocarcinoma. Other malignant tumors include mucinous cystadenocarcinoma (Fig 12), acinar cell carcinoma, islet cell tumor, and solid and papillary neoplasms (24). Benign intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms and borderline mucinous cystic tumors also have been reported (24,38). The incidence of premalignant changes in heterotopic pancreatic tissue is similar to that in the native pancreas (13). Pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasia has been found both adjacent to malignancy and in the absence of invasive cancer (13). Rendering a diagnosis preoperatively is difficult—even with endoscopic US and fine-needle aspiration biopsy, which yields diagnostic results only half of the time (37). Three criteria for the histopathologic diagnosis of tumor arising in heterotopic pancreas have been described: (a) the tumor should be near or within the heterotopic tissue, (b) there must be a direct transition between the tumor and heterotopia, and (c) the nonneoplastic heterotopic pancreatic tissue must include acini and ducts (39).

        Paraduodenal Pancreatitis

        Paraduodenal pancreatitis is an umbrella term that encompasses all fibroinflammatory processes arising in and around the minor papilla and is considered a form of focal chronic pancreatitis. Entities in this category include groove pancreatitis, cystic dystrophy of heterotopic pancreas, and pancreatic duodenal hamartoma (40). These conditions have common clinical features, imaging appearances, and outcomes and therefore may represent the same process, or they may be along the same spectrum. Although the pathogenesis of paraduodenal pancreatitis is unclear, a dominant theory is that it is caused by heterotopic pancreatic rests in the medial duodenal wall, which undergo cystic degeneration in response to chronic episodes of stress. Other theories regarding the pathogenesis of this anomaly are less well accepted and include peptic ulcer disease, gastric resection, true cysts in the duodenal wall, and abnormal flow or obstruction in the accessory pancreatic duct (20,40).

        Paraduodenal pancreatitis is most often seen in young men with a history of alcohol abuse (40). The most common presenting symptoms are abdominal pain, duodenal obstruction, and weight loss (42). There may be accompanying jaundice or an increased amylase level. Biliary strictures develop in up to 50% of cases. Histopathologic features include scarring and fibrosis in the pancreaticoduodenal groove, chronic duodenal inflammation developing into fibrosis in the duodenal wall, Brunner gland hyperplasia, and cystic lesions in the groove or duodenal wall. The cystic lesions can be true cysts or pseudocysts. The fibrosis may be confined to the groove, or it may extend into the superior aspect of the pancreatic head. Fibrosis in the duodenal wall eventually results in duodenal stenosis and obstruction, which are often early-manifesting symptoms.

        The pancreaticoduodenal groove is a potential space between the pancreatic head, common bile duct, and second portion of the duodenum (43). Groove pancreatitis can be categorized into pure and segmental forms. The pure form affects the groove exclusively and spares the pancreatic parenchyma. The segmental form predominantly affects the groove, but it also extends to involve the head of the pancreas. Although both forms spare all or much of the pancreas, they can cause chronic stenosis of the main pancreatic duct that leads to upstream stasis, dilatation, and intraductal stone formation, with resultant diffuse chronic pancreatitis (41).

        The imaging features of paraduodenal pancreatitis can be categorized into findings in the groove, duodenum, pancreas, or biliary tree. The pancreaticoduodenal groove may be widened by inflammation with indistinct fat stranding, or it may be infiltrated by soft tissue (40). The soft tissue is typically a hypovascular sheetlike mass in the groove; it narrows the duodenum and displaces the pancreatic head. With the pure form of the disease, the pancreas maintains its normal parenchymal volume and imaging characteristics. The mass is hypoattenuating relative to the avidly enhancing duodenal wall and pancreatic head at contrast-enhanced CT (Fig 13). Small cysts may be seen within the groove or mass (Fig 14) (40,41). The soft-tissue contrast at MR imaging is helpful for differentiating the mass and small cysts from the pancreas (44). The mass is hypointense relative to the normal pancreas on T1-weighted MR images. The variable signal intensity seen on T2-weighted MR images is related to the chronicity of the lesion. The mass may be edematous initially, with higher signal intensity on T2-weighted MR images, and later become hypointense owing to fibrosis. There is poor enhancement during the early phase of contrast enhancement, with progressive heterogeneous enhancement in delayed phases (Fig 15) (41,44).

        The second portion of the duodenum has medial wall thickening (>3 mm), hyperenhancement relative to the proximal jejunal loops, and small discrete cysts in the thickened wall (Fig 16). T2-weighted MR images best demonstrate the small cysts in the duodenal wall (Fig 17). These abnormalities result in luminal narrowing of the duodenum and the often early-manifesting symptom of duodenal obstruction (Fig 16). Rarely, the cysts may be large, complex, or multilocular (Fig 18).

        With the pure form of groove pancreatitis, the pancreatic parenchyma is spared; with the segmental form, however, inflammation and fibrosis may extend into the pancreatic head. Regardless of the type, peripancreatic and retroperitoneal inflammation is not a dominant feature (40). There may be pancreatic parenchymal atrophy and upstream ductal dilatation secondary to chronic inflammation and fibrosis in the groove and adjacent pancreatic head. Abnormalities of the pancreatic duct are more common with the segmental form (40). There is mild, smooth, progressive stenosis of the duct in the head of the gland (41). Pancreatic duct obstruction may be caused by ductal stricture, proteinaceous plugs, or the increased viscosity of the ductal fluid. The most common segment with ductal involvement is the accessory pancreatic duct at the minor papilla.

        Abnormalities of the biliary tree are more common with the segmental form of groove pancreatitis owing to involvement of the pancreatic head. There is smooth tapered stenosis of the common bile duct, with mild upstream biliary dilatation (40,41). The space between the duodenal lumen and common bile duct appears widened on MR cholangiopancreatographic images owing to the mass in the pancreaticoduodenal groove and thickening of the medial duodenal wall (Fig 19). The gallbladder has been described as banana shaped at MR cholangiopancreatography (41).

        Although paraduodenal pancreatitis has characteristic clinical and imaging features, care should be taken to exclude pancreatic adenocarcinoma (Table 2). Individuals with paraduodenal pancreatitis typically have a history of alcohol abuse, and the dominant presenting symptom is duodenal stenosis (45).

        With paraduodenal pancreatitis, the mass in the groove is flat, often displaces the pancreas, and is accompanied by cystic change in the groove or duodenal wall
        (44). Duodenal wall thickening, hyperenhancement, and cystic dysplasia are dominant features of paraduodenal pancreatitis and typically are absent with adenocarcinoma (41,45). Additional differentiating findings that suggest paraduodenal pancreatitis are the absence of adenopathy and distant metastases and the lack of vascular encasement or invasion of adjacent organs (40). In the setting of paraduodenal pancreatitis, biliary strictures are commonly smooth and tapered compared with the typical abrupt shouldering stenosis seen with adenocarcinoma. Upstream biliary dilatation is mild, and jaundice is either absent or a nondominant feature. Imaging is helpful for narrowing the differential diagnosis and favoring a benign diagnosis; however, endoscopic or laparoscopic biopsy may be needed to exclude adenocarcinoma (40).

        Conclusion

        Heterotopic pancreas is a congenital anomaly that is rarely identified prospectively at imaging; rather, most cases are identified at surgery or autopsy. Knowledge of the most common locations and characteristic imaging appearances of heterotopic pancreatic tissue is key to rendering the diagnosis preoperatively. Associated complications include pancreatitis, pseudocyst formation, malignant degeneration, gastrointestinal bleeding, bowel obstruction, and intussusception. Paraduodenal pancreatitis in particular should be considered in the differential diagnosis of periampullary disease.
